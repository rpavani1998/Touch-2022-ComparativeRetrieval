HIGHER EDUCATION
Columbia College bolsters appeal with online classes

By ABRAHAM MAHSHIE of the Tribune’s staff Published Sunday, June 22, 2008


Gerik Parmele photo
Ernie Wren, an adjunct professor at Columbia College, prepares class materials at the school’s online campus building. Wren owns a property management company and teaches management and small business over the Web. The college has 8,000 online students taking 12,000 courses.

Columbia College has ridden the Internet wave to establish itself as one of the largest and best values in online education, but this year, administrators are talking about how to beef up the day and night campuses, too.

“Looking into the future, we want to become the model institution of our type that combines a number of venues — in classroom or in-seat and online — while making ourselves qualitatively better and more flexible,” said Gerard Brouder, who is in his 13th year as president of the private liberal arts school.

Columbia College has started a five-year program to increase the day-campus enrollment by 20 percent, to 1,250, by 2012.

“We were looking at some of the liberal arts majors at the college, and several of them had some room for growth,” said Terry Smith, executive vice president and dean of academic affairs.

While other local schools have cut back or plan to scale back faculty and programs, Smith said Columbia College is gearing up for an increase in faculty to maintain a student-to-faculty ratio of 14:1. In fact, Columbia College does not have classrooms capable of seating more than 30 students.

“I think it’s in good part because of the increased reputation of the school,” Smith said, referring to enrollment growth of 30 to 50 students per year for several years. “We have a solid product, and we’ve been able to grow and increase quality.”

Smith said Columbia College has stepped up marketing and international recruiting, boasting 100 international students from 25 countries last year.

Brouder and Smith also said creating new programs to meet market needs has increased students from outside Missouri.

“We were the first to establish an undergraduate forensic science program in the state,” Smith said. “We have established a very fine reputation for that.”

Smith said the program’s popularity is a result of the television series “CSI,” and it prepares students for jobs as criminal lab technicians and investigators.

“It’s very heavily a science degree,” he advised. “It’s not just criminal justice plus intro to biology. There is lots of biology and chemistry.”

Greater interest in physical sciences has prompted the school to plan for a new $14 million science building to replace its 1960s-era building that formerly was an infirmary. The school has already raised $1 million.

Columbia College plans to continue playing to its strength of being at the cutting edge of online education. At any given time, 8,000 students are enrolled in 12,000 courses. Seven years ago, there were 180 students taking 10 classes.

“We feel the quality of the education for an online course is as good — I hate to use the words — if not better … than traditional,” said Gary Massey, associate dean of Adult Higher Education & Online Campus, who said the online campus is growing by 20 percent a year.

Massey and others contend that part of the online success is understanding that day, night and online courses have different students and require different learning and teaching styles.

“They’re very different populations. They are taught differently, and they learn differently,” Brouder said. That often means tenured, full-time professors teaching traditional students while adjunct faculty who are working professionals teach online and adult night classes.

“Our students are working adults. They want to get a degree for that next promotion,” Massey said. “Almost all our adjuncts are working, practicing professionals.”

And those professionals are savvy about what they are spending their money on.

“We charge much less than many of our competitors,” Brouder said. “We are able to do it because we are a non-profit. … Our bottom line is meant for the sole purpose of re-investing it into the infrastructure and personnel of the institution.”

Brouder said Columbia College has another advantage over other local schools such as the University of Missouri.

“We are a private institution. We report to a board. We don’t run into politics,” said Brouder, who worked at MU for 18 years before joining Columbia College, which he believes is a model hybrid institution.

“How do you know you become a model?” he asked rhetorically. “By how many institutions are coming to us, or ask us to come to them to guide them on the path that Columbia College has taken with variable delivery modes and various venues and has a singular mission: teaching and learning.”
