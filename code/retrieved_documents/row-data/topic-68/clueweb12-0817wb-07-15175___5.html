Physical Therapists

Physical Therapists Job Description

Physical therapists have an extremely important job. Human health is complex and unpredictable for the most part, so people must rely on the expertise of health care professionals to care for them when they are ill or injured. While doctors may take care of the sick and injured initially, it is the duty of physical therapists to ensure that those who were severely injured or recovering for a long period of time are able to regain their strength and coordination in order to complete routine activities. Physical therapists examine each patient and develop treatment plans for them depending on the severity of their condition and the likelihood of recovery. Many physical therapists work with physicians to determine what treatments can and cannot be done, and they keep records of patient progress. Treatments that are not yielding the desired results are typically reformed so that they may be more beneficial. These therapists typically work in hospitals, outpatient clinics, and physician offices. Each work day can be physically demanding as they must help patients run, walk, stand, or sit.

Physical Therapists Degree Requirements

The training to become a physical therapist is intensive and requires commitment as well as hard work. All physical therapists must have at least a master’s degree in physical therapy, which typically takes about two to three years after first earning a four-year bachelor’s degree. Better employment opportunities will arise for those who earn a doctoral degree in the subject, however, which typically takes about three years to complete in addition to a bachelor’s degree. During the degree program, students will take courses in anatomy, biology, exercise physiology, radiology and imaging, and clinical reasoning. Some courses in behavioral science and therapeutic interventions would be beneficial to aspiring physical therapists as well. After finishing a master’s degree program, students can apply to their state for licensure, which may require the completion and passing of a standardized examination. Interning at a hospital or clinic would also be useful to help boost a prospective physical therapist’s resume as well.

Why Physical Therapy is a Hot Career

Health care in general is experiencing a renewed boom in interest and popularity. Out of all of the industries, health care is showing the best signs of prosperity. This may be due to the fact that the need for health care professionals will never go away because of the unpredictable nature of health and injury, which drives up the demand for physical therapists. Employment opportunities for physical therapists are projected to increase 30% during the 2008-18 decade, according to theU.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS). This will add approximately 56,200 more jobs into the market, not including the positions that will be open due to retirement or career change. The median annual salary for physical therapists was $72,790 in 2008, the latest information available from the BLS. However, even the lowest 10% of physical therapists earned a hefty salary with an average of less than $50,350 per year.
