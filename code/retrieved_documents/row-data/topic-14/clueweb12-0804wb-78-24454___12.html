Elective Memory Elective Memory
Posted by tnolan at 29/1/2009 5:14 PM GMTon bmj.com


Was my elective the best eight weeks of medical school? It is supposed to be – it’s the big carrot that is waved in front of you for five years to get you through the embryology and histology lectures. For me though, looking back after three years, I can’t help but feel that I could have made more out of it.

 

There are two main ways to approach your elective. You can go with some friends with the aim of clearing off after a couple of weeks in a hospital and learn a foreign language or how to surf. Alternatively, you can immerse yourself in the healthcare of a truly foreign environment. I think I got caught in the middle of these two.

 

I went to Fiji and spent six weeks on the beautiful island of Levuka and the following two weeks exploring the rest of the islands. I spent my days at a small hospital run by three doctors. In the mornings there was a ward round and clinic. The wards were quite empty in general as anything serious was helicoptered out to the mainland. The clinics were a different story, but interestingly the most common thing I saw was hypertension, which is a huge problem in Fiji thanks to an unfortunate combination of genetic predisposition and fondness for fast foods. The treatments available weren’t great – they got their supplies from charities based in New Zealand and when they ran out of one thing they just had to move to another.

 

January in Fiji is a special time of year for many schoolchildren. When they reach about eight years old it is traditional to be circumcised. Many of the local parents were very keen to have their children circumcised by the “white doctor” and I was glad to get a bit more involved in things (most of the locals didn’t speak much English so out-patients soon lost its novelty). I still enjoy telling my non-doctor friends about these operations and seeing the looks of horror on their faces.

 

The doctors and staff at the hospital were all fantastic. They were extremely welcoming and friendly and would take me out in the evenings and at weekends. The locals soon got to recognise me on the street and would shout “ennui”, meaning doctor, as took my daily walks to and from the hospital along a road with beautiful views out to sea.

 

“What do you mean you can’t help but feel that you could have made more out of it?” I hear you gasp, perhaps with a “you malcontent” at the end. Well, if I was to go again I think I would try harder to push myself. I stayed at The Royal Hotel, a beautiful old colonial hotel frequented by backpackers. I had plenty of opportunities to get more experience of the local culture but every time I would decline and go and hang out with the backpackers. I also spent far too long reading a textbook as the fear of finals was growing.

 

So if you’re about to go on your elective and you’re going so you can experience a different culture, my advice would be to resist the temptation to hang out with people from back home, and get off the tourist trail.

I enjoyed reading your experiences Tom.
I went to New York for my elective and did a programme run and funded by Columbia University based in the lower East Side of Manhattan on Urban medicine, immigrant health care and palliative care. I shadowed a doctor who worked for St Vincents and went to clinics with her to Hostels for homeless people off Times Square, women refuges, HIV clinics and clinics for people with serious menatl health problems off Wall Street and palliative care (in the Bronx). I was the only Brit in a group of final year medical students and we met 4 nights a week for seminars. I experienced a part of New York you'd never normally get to to see. I leaned alot from my experiences and remain passionate about the city
I have 2 good friends in New York as a result and reflect on my experiences quite frequently -given it's now 10 years ago.
My advice would be simply to choose an elective experience that enables you to to experience and learn something new / different -that won't be so easy later on! Sunshine would have been nice-but I enjoyed experiencing New York in the snow.

lornap wrote:
I am so jealous of your NYC electives would have loved to do mine there! Having said that I thoroughly enjoyed my placements last summer. I chose to go to Los Angeles for five weeks followed by a month in Belize. Two very different locations! The genres of medicine I observed were also polar opposites.

I arrived in LA on the Sunday and somehow navigated the bus system through the second largest city in the States on a Monday morning to Santa Monica. I was attached to the Integrative East-West Medical Centre, an air-conditioned glass and white walled haven of a workplace. And the medicine? A fusion of traditional Chinese and high tech Western Medicine. Ok ok so it might sound a little "LA" but the combination was really appealing to me as someone who would always prefer to try something natural and less toxic that some of the things we're prescribed. The pacific ocean half a mile away didn’t hurt either! The clinicians were all MD's who had also trained in Traditional Chinese Medicine (for another three years at least!). Impressive teachers.

I learnt acupuncture, cupping, acupressure massage and trigger point injections (a kind of supersized western acupuncture). I even mastered the basics of reading tongues. Contrary to what I was informed prior to arrival I was actually encouraged to take a hands on approach. I was allowed to clerk patients and help administer therapies and was really involved in the team. This surprised me as the picture older students had painted of American electives involved being made to stand in the corner with your hands tied for fear of being sued. I organised the placement through UCLA, they literally have hundreds of courses available in any area you can imagine-HIGHLY RECOMMENDED!

I then went on to southern and extremely rural Belize in Central America-you can imagine the culture shock. Common conditions changed over night from laptop induced neck pain and headaches only relieved by Botox to scabies and diarrhoea. The whole two months were truly brilliant and I do think this had something to do with the enormous variety in the locations I chose.
