[Video] To members, goat co-op is most rewarding way to get milk

Amore, Morphea, Tulip and Crystal are always excited to have visitors. As you approach them, they jump and turn in little circles, and once you're close enough, they strain to stick their faces through the chicken-wire fence, because they want to sniff your hands and gnaw on your sleeves. These girls are curious, friendly and almost eerily smart. A hand-painted sign above their pen proudly declares where you are: The Goat Co-Op.

The Goat Co-Op -- which is also known as Miel y Leche -- is about a year and a half old and located off West Alameda, on land past Siler Road where property lines become unclear and farm-animal sightings more common. Miel y Leche is the second goat collective off of West Alameda, and joins an established co-op known as The West Alameda Goat Milkers, just a short drive up the road. The two co-ops aren't affiliated, but they share a love of goats and local food.

The Goat Milkers have been around for 15 years and house three goats: Itsy, Reba and Soufflé. On a recent weekday afternoon, Goat Milkers member and local lawyer Cordelia Friedman walked up to the pen where the goats live. Everyone was happy to see one another. "Like all modern animals, they know the sound of a plastic bag," Friedman said as she readied treats for the animals. "Come see why carrots are the crack cocaine of the goat world," she joked. Itsy, Soufflé and Reba crowded against one another and strained their necks to gobble carrot sticks from Friedman's outstretched hands. "Reba is especially naughty because she's a teenager," Friedman said as she stroked Reba's head.

"I didn't know anything about goats when we got started, but I was willing to try," said Mike Nestor, a founding member of Miel y Leche who lives on the co-op property with his wife and two children. The learning curve has been steep, he said, but enjoyable, too.

Nestor is a builder and led construction of the goats' living quarters. At both co-ops, the goats live alongside flocks of chickens, and at Miel y Leche, a turkey, too. "Goats are strong animals, they're active, and they're smart and social," Nestor said. "The design of the pens needs to be tight. It's really interesting to watch them together; they have complex relationships."

Amore is Tulip's mother, and Morphea is Crystal's, and the design of the pens reflects these relationships. Generally, the two younger goats are kept together in one pen while their mothers are in another. This arrangement discourages nursing, although each kid can be paired with an older goat that's not its mother -- goats don't allow kids who aren't their offspring to nurse.

Miel y Leche and Goat Milkers each have about 10 members, made up of individuals and families. At both co-ops, members share the costs of owning goats, which include feed (grain if they're lactating or pregnant, hay if they're not) vet visits and stud fees. (At Goat Milkers, Soufflé is pregnant, and Miel y Leche hopes that both Tulip and Crystal are, too.)

Decision-making is a group process, and deliberations vary widely: from what kind of feed to use, whether to opt for homeopathy or Western medicine when the goats get sick, and what to do when a male goat is born. "[Members] don't have to be like-minded, but you do have to figure out how you're going to deal with these things," Nestor said.

Members of both co-ops take on different responsibilities that suit their skills and interests. Everyone contributes to tasks like mucking the pens, maintaining fencing and structures, and making sure all members are up to date on everything goaty, whether in a written logbook or a Google Group.

Nobody joins a co-op for a cheap goat-milk fix. "For sure, it's much easier to go to the store and buy goat's milk," said Alessandra Haines, who, along with her husband, Steve, is a member of Miel y Leche. "But part of the experience is taking care of them and learning about them."

"People say goat cheese is so expensive, and you're like, have you ever milked a goat?" Friedman said. Milking is tricky at first (really -- it's not as easy as it looks) and Friedman said new co-op members practice air-milking to build up hand strength.

Many goat experiences are learn-as-you-go. Haines, for instance, is in charge of trimming the goats' hooves. "My friend the farrier taught me how," she said, and lifted Morphea's leg to demonstrate how to trim excess growth along the edge of the hoof with garden shears.

The benefits of belonging to a cooperative are at once obvious and hard-to-define. There's the physical product, about a half-gallon of milk per goat per day. Goats are milked twice daily, in the early morning and in the evening. Members drink the milk -- especially if they have young children -- and can choose to pasteurize it or not. Many people make cheese, kefir and yogurt. Nestor said his summer plans include experimenting with ice cream. Belonging to a co-op has benefits beyond the milk, too, like control over what goes into the goats, learning new skills and the ultimate in localized production: making food from start to finish by yourself.

"Building and maintaining relationships with your food is weirdly rewarding," Haines said. "Whether it's a garden or chickens or goats, the ongoing process of taking care of and investing energy in food production heightens appreciation of our interdependent relationships with animals and plants. And everything tastes better!"

Goat co-ops are DIY in action, farmyard experiential learning, started by adventurous folks who are willing to try something new. But raising goats isn't always easy. One of Goat Milkers' newborn kids died last year, and Tulip, one of Miel y Leche's younger goats, has long, sharp -- and potentially dangerous -- horns because they weren't cauterized early enough.

"It's not about us and how cute we think they are," Friedman said. "We've learned a lot -- you get a little less starry-eyed about husbandry. It doesn't always comport with your ideas about how you think things should go."

Nestor echoed this statement. "Animal husbandry can be pretty harsh," he said. "There's a certain number of people who get into co-ops that aren't ready for that. For instance, you need to cauterize the nubs that their horns grow from pretty early on. I was holding Crystal when the vet did it; it's like being next to a barbecue. [The animal] is in a ton of pain, the vet's going through layers of flesh to get to the skull. But then the goat doesn't get horns."

Despite setbacks and unknowns, each co-op is thriving. Baby goats are expected this summer, and members are committed both to the goats (which they love) and to thinking carefully about local food sources.

"Once you get to know the goats and care for them, you get a sense of responsibility, and that's appropriate," Friedman said. "You can't let the goats go unmilked or unfed. Everyone has to chip in during the hardest times. It takes a lot to keep them happy and healthy."

Belonging to a co-op indicates a deliberate, deep engagement in community and with food. "I like good food, but I'm not a moralist about it," Nestor said. "But, I do think it's good for us all to know more about what we eat, and to enjoy it more. One way to do this is to produce your own."

Of course, goat love is hardly a side-benefit of co-op membership. Watching Soufflé, Itsy and Reba frolic in the sun, Friedman leaned in conspiratorially. "People do get very smitten with the goats," she said.
