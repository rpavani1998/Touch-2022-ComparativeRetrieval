In the Drugstore: OTC Medications to Reduce Fever -- What You Need to Know for You and Your Child

From Maureen Salamon, 

Updated February 24, 2009

About.com Health's Disease and Condition content is reviewed by the Medical Review Board

Filed In:
  1. Drugs

(LifeWire) - It's hard to beat the convenience of over-the-counter (OTC) medications, especially when a family member needs quick relief from a fever. But just because OTC drugs don't require a doctor's approval doesn't mean choosing them can be done carelessly. Fever reducers need to target each person's age and weight, among other factors, to be as effective and safe as possible.

Scanning store shelves when looking for fever reducers can be dizzying because so many brands and dosages are available. But don't let the wide array of packaging fool you: Only two classes of OTC medications are designed specifically to lower fever, which is defined as 100.4 F or higher in infants. (Normal body temperatures vary among individuals and with the time of day.)

Products Containing Acetaminophen: This is the first class of OTC medication designed to lower a fever. It is found in brands such as Tylenol and Anacin-3 (acetaminophen). It is also an active ingredient in more than 600 drugs, both prescription and OTC. This class of medicine includes not only fever reducers, but also pain relievers, cough suppressants and cold remedies.

Nonsteroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs): These are better known in their abbreviated form, as NSAIDs, and these are the second class of OTC meds. These are also used primarily for fever and pain relief. Meds with this active ingredient within this class include the brand names of Advil and Motrin (ibuprofen), Aleve (naproxen sodium) or common aspirin.

Both drug classes have venerable safety records for fever reduction, but aspirin should never be used for this purpose in children under 19 years of age, who are more vulnerable to a deadly disease known as Reye's syndrome. The cause and cure of Reye's syndrome are still unknown, but its development in children is widely linked to the use of aspirin, which contains the chemical salicylate. (Check the labels. This chemical is sometimes listed in different forms, including acetylsalicylate, acetylsalicylic acid and salicylic acid.)

Children with colds or viruses, including chicken pox, who are given aspirin can experience the rapid onset of Reye's, whose symptoms include:

  • Listlessness
  • Ongoing vomiting
  • Confusion
  • Lethargy
  • Aggressive or irrational behavior

If you suspect your child is developing Reye's syndrome, which can occur even without aspirin ingestion, then seek emergency medical care immediately.

The age of the person taking an OTC fever reducer is important in choosing the appropriate type. So is their weight. Both acetaminophen and NSAIDs are available in a variety of forms and dosages to satisfy these important criteria.

Liquid medicines, often designed for children, are typically packaged with a small cup, dropper or cylindrical spoon to increase dosage accuracy. Don't use household spoons to administer medication, since they are rarely equivalent to the teaspoon or tablespoon dose advised on the package.

Tablets and caplets, usually aimed at adults, come in various shapes and sizes, as well as dose levels. Fever reducers also can be taken rectally as suppositories, which is useful if the patient is vomiting and cannot ingest anything by mouth.

Be sure to read the label of any medicine you or a family member is about to take. All OTC medication labels list the active ingredient(s), what symptoms the product helps, who it's for, how much to take and how often. Labels also state if the product is safe for children over 2. The US Food and Drug Administration recommends that OTC cough and cold medicines not be used for those less than 2 years old and are not advised for those under 4. However, single-ingredient OTC fever reducers such as acetaminophen and ibuprofen are approved for infants, though parents are advised to consult a doctor regarding the correct dosage for those under 2.

What else to watch for when using these meds? Unintentional overdoses of fever reducers are quite common because their active ingredients are duplicated in other common OTC medications. For instance, a cold sufferer may take a multi-symptom cold medicine containing acetaminophen along with headache medicine containing the same active ingredient, essentially receiving a double dose, without even realizing it.

Additionally, these medications can become toxic, even at correct dosages, among young children suffering from dehydration due to fever, vomiting and/or diarrhea.

Excess levels of acetaminophen or NSAIDs are not harmless. The risk of liver damage increases in those who drink three or more alcoholic beverages each day while taking ongoing doses of acetaminophen, and emergency rooms see many who took much more than the recommended dose and ended up with a life-threatening case of acetaminophen poisoning. NSAIDs can cause stomach bleeding or kidney problems, especially in senior citizens and those on certain medications.

Check with your doctor if you have any questions about which OTC fever reducers are right for you.
