    "omne trium perfectum?"

    Dec 19, 2011  

    What an unlikely trio. Or not?
    On December 15th, Christopher Hitchens, the contrarian polemicist, got to wherever it is that we all get to go - first.

    Hitchen's wasn't everyone's cup of tea but he might have appealed, at least weakly, at some level.

    On the 17th, Vaclav Havel of the Velvet Revolution, playwright turned activist and the architect of the Czech republic followed.

    Havel appears to have been much revered, though seems less instantly recognizable. Perhaps his ego was better managed as popularity waned, after his modern-day-democratic miracle. Celebrity, however, is relative and context dependent. It's unlikely that Hitchens is being mourned in Prague, in quite the same way as Havel.


    And then, this morning, we learned that Kim Jong Il, North Korea's secretive, Communist dictator relic had also joined the club. Curiously, this was reported some two days after the fact. As is clear with everything about that murky place, the balance betwixt celebration and mourning is not easily calibrated.


    I suspect that Hitchens and Havel may have shared sensibilities. Though these were expressed in vastly different forms, their tendencies appear to have died right alongside them.  As for Kim Jong Il, his assertion that "great ideology creates great times" remains inexplicable, outside of the totalitarian bubble he presided over in a reincarnation of his own father, Kim Il-Sung. Now, his legend appears set to live on in Kim Jong-un, his equally unpredictable great successor. Hitchens had earlier imagined such a "necrocratic trinity".


    As different as they were, I find it fitting to quote from Tom Rachman's brilliant debut novel The Imperfectionists. This might apply to all three:


    "I say that ambition is absurd and yet I remain in its thrall... from my earliest childhood it dominated me. I longed for achievements, to be influential - that, in particular. To sway people. This has been my religion: the belief that I deserve attention, that they are wrong not to listen, that those who dispute me are fools. Yet, no matter what I achieve, the world lives on, impertinent indifferent.  Here is a fact: nothing in all civilization has been as productive and ludicrous as ambition. Whatever its ills, nothing has created more. Cathedrals, sonata's, encyclopedias: love of God was not behind them, not love of life. But the love of man to be worshipped by man...."


    There's a queue forming at the great humidor in the sky.
    I hear they all smoked like chimneys.

    Who could ever think that God doesn't have a sense of humour?
