Brilliant Marketing or What? The New Coke Story

Posted by John Grafton on Tue, Sep 20, 2011 @ 08:00 AM   
 Email Article   
Tweet   

  
  

Once a month, The Marketing Dialog examines a marketing campaign and asks the question, “Brilliant Marketing or What?”

This month’s subject came from a very hard dose of reality I encountered last week when I was speaking with a junior member of our staff and mentioned New Coke.  I got that ‘deer caught in the headlights’ look that told me she had never heard of New Coke, nor probably had anyone in her generation since this all happened in the mid-80’s.  So for you who are old enough to remember, take a trip down memory lane with me as I distill the New Coke experience, with the benefit of 20/20 hindsight, for the younger researchers.

It should be noted that before New Coke was introduced, Pepsi was conducting its very successful marketing campaign ‘The Pepsi Challenge’ that compared Coke to Pepsi. In the taste-test challenges, Pepsi usually won but not for the right reasons.  Respondents were offered a cold, fresh cup of Pepsi next to a lukewarm, flat cup of Coke.  Which would you choose?  With so many dollars at stake in the soda category, it isn’t a surprise that research methodologies are occasionally skewed.

Coke market share began declining and the company embarked on extensive marketing research to determine the correct response. Based on many, many focus groups and taste tests, Coke concluded that the answer was solely the taste of the product.  The taste tests were conducted offering respondents a choice between a small cup of regular Coke, a small cup of a sweeter formula of Coke and a small cup of Pepsi that resulted in the roll-out of New Coke.  The sweeter Coke usually was preferred. However, although a small cup of a sweeter drink may taste better at the time, that doesn’t necessarily mean that a 12 ounce ‘sweeter’ drink would be preferred.  Too much of a sweet thing might not be what Coke drinkers wanted. 

Nonetheless, Coke marketers’ faith in their taste tests was so strong, since the sweeter mixture beat both Pepsi and ‘regular’ Coke, New Coke was introduced in 1985 to replace the original drink formula. New Coke was immediately put into production and the original drink was completely discontinued.

The public’s reaction to New Coke – or more accurately to the discontinuation of the original Coke formula - was sudden and severe.  Although market research showed that 80 percent of the American public was aware of the change within days and the company’s stock went up after the announcement, that wasn’t necessarily a good thing.  The mistake made was treating Coke as any other brand and not as a true American icon.

Although most tasters liked the sweeter New Coke formula, 10 percent - 12 percent of consumers stated that they were angry that Coke would consider changing the original recipe and some went so far as to state that they would stop drinking Coke altogether rather than switch to Pepsi.  Anti-New Coke groups sprang up overnight, most vocal in the South (home of Coca-Cola), and it soon became apparent that consumers were more upset about the withdrawal of the original formula than pleased with the taste of the new one.  In fact, these consumers told researchers that it felt like they were discussing the death of a family member versus a soft drink.

The switch lasted just 77 days until Coca-Cola announced it was bringing back Coke ‘Classic.’ Even then, however, consumers were suspicious of Coke and didn’t think Classic tasted like the original recipe. They were right since Coke had substituted cane sugar in the original formula for high-fructose corn syrup, which gives the drink a different taste (but is less expensive for the company to use in manufacturing the product.)  

Adding to the confusion, immediately after the switch, ‘conspiracy’ rumors flew that Coke had done this as a marketing ploy to increase sales in the original brand when it was reintroduced.  In response to this, Donald Keogh, Coke’s president at the time stated, “We’re not that dumb, and we’re not that smart”. 

What Coke failed to anticipate was the visceral connection consumers felt to the product they grew up with.  The Coke marketers finally concluded was that it hadvastly underestimated the size of the public who would be alienated by the switch, even when introduced to a new product they perceived was preferred. 

By 2002, Coke II, as it was eventually called, was discontinued entirely.  In a strange twist, however, if you look at the soda shelves these days, you will see ‘retro’ soda formulations, such as the ‘original’ Mountain Dew and ‘original’ Pepsi being offered to consumers.  The primary difference in the ‘original’ versus the current formulations is that the ‘retro’ versions use sugar, not corn syrup as the sweetener.  Since it’s been over 25 years since New Coke debuted, maybe it’s time to try it as a brand extension for their existing core products. However, they should definitely not call it New Coke! 

Should we give New Coke a second chance or relegate it to the long list of case studies of products that failed?  Do you think the research conducted was faulty – or just not thorough enough?
