Comparison: 2010 Ford Fusion Hybrid vs 2009 Toyota Camry Hybrid

Your Mileage Will Definitely Vary
 

2010 Ford Fusion Buyer's Guide


MSRP: N/A

MPG Range: 18 - 36 mpg

Body Style: Sedan

From the January, 2009 issue of Motor Trend
By Kim Reynolds
 | Photos Brian Vance Tweet
|  



 Click to view Gallery

Although the EPA has come a long way in rehabilitating its reputation as a creditable source of mileage numbers, that newfound respectability could be suffering a hiccup with the all-new,2010 Ford Fusion Hybrid. What's the buzz about? Two numbers -- 41 and 36 -- which the government has certified as the midsizeFord's official city and highway ratings. Trouble is, our early observations have theFusion's overall number floating around 33.5 mpg. Hey, that's an excellent result in our book. Except that it leaves a yawning, 5-mpg disparity betwixt it and the government's combined average of 38.5.

So what's up with the Fusion? I called Praveen Cherian, Ford's Hybrid Program leader to find out

"So, Kim, what mileage did you get...?"

"Umm, about 33 and a half." Silence. Darn, there goes getting added to Ford's Christmas card list yet again.

"So what kind of driving was that?" Praveen calmly followed up.


 Click to view Gallery

"About 500 miles of typical mixed use -- highway driving, city driving; you know, everything you'd do with a car." I sensed a vibe of disappointment and decided to change the subject.

In retrospect, though, there was nothing to be disappointed about. While our early experience suggests the real-world mileages of the Fusion and gold-standardCamry are closer than Ford might like, that doesn't mean the Fusion still isn't the better hybrid (and potentially capable of super mileage).

Better? It seems that hybrid buyers have now established a set of experiential expectations for what a "good hybrid" is. Requirments? It should start up exclusively in EV mode. Shut its engine down very quickly when it decelerates. Operate as an EV up to as high a speed as possible in the city and have its engine's relighting be unnoticeable. As Mr. Cherian points out, the primary benefit of a hybrid is its recapture of kinetic energy otherwise lost to braking. And then reusing it. On-board generation of electricity for filling in the engine's torque holes and whatnot, offers a smaller bang for the technological buck (the electricity's chemical storage and recovery involve not-inconsiderable losses). Notably, the pressure to meet these hybrid expectations is now so high that manufacturers will knowingly trade a little efficiency to deliver those experiential goods.

And simply put, the Fusion delivers those subjective goods better than any hybrid on the market. Compared with theCamry Hybrid, its willingness to remain in EV mode is delightful (up to 47 mph while tickling the accelerator -- see our graphic of measurements gleaned from OBD2 port to prove it). The volume level of its EV-audio experience has just enough whine to provide that vital soundtrack of efficiency. But it's not so loud as to be annoying. And the trunk space is also noticeably less impacted by its battery pack.
