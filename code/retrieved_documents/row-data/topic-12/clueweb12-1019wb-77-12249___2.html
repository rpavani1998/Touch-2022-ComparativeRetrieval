Are greener planes an acceptable compromise?

20 Jun 2007 | Author: Tobias Webb | Print version | Send to a friend

Check in here for lower emissions

While aviation retains its bad-boy image, at least one airline is dealing with the public’s concerns about climate change

It seems like an odd concept in the face of the UK’s current anti-flying hysteria, but picking the most environmentally friendly airline could be a way for consumers to engage with climate change while governments wrestle with tougher questions.

Flybe, a UK airline, says is has now invested more than $2 billion in “environmentally sensitive” aircraft. By 2009, they say, they will have one of the most environmentally friendly aircraft fleets in the world.

Campaigners will be up in arms about this. Newer groups like the articulate and direct action focused Plane Stupid organisation have already been on television news complaining about a recent report from an airline pilots union.

Rail’s halo slips…

The British Airline Pilots Association claims that for flights over 500 kilometres, new planes perform as well as trains.

Plane Stupid retorts that airline emissions are two to four times more harmful to the environment at altitude, and that most flights are less than 500 kilometres in Europe anyway, making the train a better option.

Flybe says their “ecolabel” initiative for their new planes is “designed to help consumers identify which type of aircraft or which type of route is the most sensitive choice to the environment”.


"Effective insulation in homes would get bigger gains, quicker, than fighting flying"


The airline is also going to buy carbon offsets via PURE, a non-profit carbon offsetter. Passengers will opt in to pay for these.

Flybe’s self created eco-labelling scheme will be audited by Deloitte, and will provide passengers with “detailed but user-friendly breakdown of the fuel consumption, carbon emissions and noise patterns of the aircraft type to be used on their journey”.

The scheme will allow passengers to assess the environmental impact of their journey. Passengers, the airline says, “will then be in a position to decide, on an informed basis, whether they want to carbon-offset that journey”.

Flying: a reality

Flybe pitches its new planes as scoring “an impressive B rating for CO2 emissions”.

It is hard to know if that’s impressive or not. However, in a low margin industry, where trade publications still refer to environmental “myths”, such as climate change, this seems to signify leadership.

For some environmentalists, flying will now never be socially acceptable. For everyone else, it will continue to be a reality for longer distances.

Governments and companies appear to be hoping that if we can get emissions down elsewhere, that will have to make up for it. Even Kyoto excludes it. Carbon offsetters too, will probably do well out of it in the coming years, although there is bound to be a shake out in the market over time.

Given that effective insulation in homes would get bigger gains, quicker, than fighting flying, perhaps its time for the “insulation action group” to be formed.

Water, transport, food, heating, and power are all going to be affected by climate change and its change agenda. The action by governments right now is focusing on the easy wins: pressuring business, for example, or pushing product carbon labelling.

But soon much harder questions, like fuel prices and their impact on behaviour will need addressing if the 70 or 80% cuts in carbon currently suggested are anything like realistic.
