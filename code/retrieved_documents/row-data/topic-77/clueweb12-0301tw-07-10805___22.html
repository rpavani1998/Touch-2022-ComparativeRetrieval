    Fear of an obesity epidemic is making the problem worse | Jenny Jameson

    This article was published on guardian.co.uk at 10.41 GMT on Wednesday 22 February 2012.
'The idea that we simply cut consumption by X number of calories isn't leading to the expected benefits.' Photograph: Robert Llewelyn/Getty Images

I'm not surprised to read the latest news that there has been an overstatement on how long it will take people to lose weight. Anyone who has tried even one diet will be familiar with the dieters' plateau. And simply cutting back on calories won't work in the long term because the body willslow its metabolic rate. This has been suspected for some time, so why we are still applauding television programmes like The Biggest Loser, where people lose large amounts of weight each week? This show has come in forcriticism in the past because of its unrealistic portrayal of weight loss and such unrealistic expectations just create anxiety.

You're following all the rules, giving it your all and yet the scales turn round and blow a raspberry at you. Your heart sinks, you feel the shame and disappointment rising and you look in the mirror to start on the blame and recriminations.

"If I'd just shut my gaping gob, all would be OK."

"I shouldn't have blown off that Zumba class to go out with my friends."

"I'm such a useless failure."

If any of this sounds familiar, I'd like you to ask yourself a few questions. How useful is all this self-blame? Does it make you feel good about yourself? Do you feel confident and happy? And wasn't feeling confident and happy one of the main reasons you wanted to lose weight in the first place?

This fear of a fat planet is just feeding stress and I think just making the problem worse.

Continually beating ourselves up because we don't fit in with official guidelines isn't doing us any good. Instead we think: "I'm obviously not losing this weight as I should, so I may as well give in and stuff my face." When we can't live up to our expectations we are more likely to engage in behaviour that can lead to poor health – eating large amounts of junk food in a short space of time.

In fact, I think the whole weight-centred approach to health is extremely unhelpful. It stigmatises people who don't fit into the "acceptable" range, regardless of whether these people are healthy or not. To hold weight loss up as the path to health can bedangerous.

Instead of demonising people for being fat we should start focussing instead on health.Linda Bacon and Lucy Aphramor have done some great work in this area and I'd love to see more health professionals take this approach. We may see a reduction in obesity rates, we may not – but isn't it the obesity-related illnesses we really want to reduce, rather than simply berating people because their bodies don't fit in with guidelines, which are currently in doubt?

We cannot continue to take a one-size-fits-all approach to health. The idea that we simply cut consumption by X number of calories isn't leading to the expected benefits. Could it be possible that we don't know precisely what causes the body to lose weight and maintain that loss? One day, perhaps, we will, but in the meantime do we really want to sacrifice our health in order to pursue a goal of weight loss that may well be a blind alley?

If someone is eating a lot of junk food and not exercising then it is going to lead to poor health, but there are many cases of people who have not achieved a so-called healthy weight but whose other health readings – blood pressure and cholesterol for instance –are within a healthy range.

So please, let's stop putting the emphasis on the numbers on the scales and start using other measurements for health. Were we to do this I would expect to see more healthy and happy people on this planet. I'm sure that's something everyone would agree is a worthwhile pursuit.

    Muscle never, ever, turns to fat. What happens is that people stop exercising but do not reduce their calorific intake. result, more energy in and less energy out, so they get fat.
