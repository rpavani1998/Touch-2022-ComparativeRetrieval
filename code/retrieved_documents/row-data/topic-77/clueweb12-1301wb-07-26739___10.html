A Guide to Healthy Cooking

By Erin · July 6, 2010 · 7 Comments
Food, Health, Vitamins · Tagged: cooking, diet, Vitamins, weight loss

Many people know which foods are essentially “healthy” and which are not.  Far fewer consider the impact that food preparation has on the nutritional value of food  and its implications for long term health.

When it comes to eating a healthy diet, in addition to making wise choices about which foods to eat, it is equally important to consider how best to cook them.  The truth is, that certain cooking techniques (like steaming) are simply “more healthy” than others.  It is also true that certain cookware (like iron, certified ceramics, enamel and glass) transfer fewer potentially harmful materials into the foods you cook using them.

When you heat food in any type of pot or cookware, there is a tendency for the materials from which the pot is made to enter the food.  Aluminum, iron, stainless steel, copper and ceramics are all popular materials for manufacturing cookware.  When you cook using them, you inadvertently end up contaminating your food with small amounts of the material from which they are made.  To minimize risks from cookware contamination, you should follow a few simple rules:

- Don’t store or cook foods for long periods in aluminum cookware
- Don’t store highly acidic foods, like tomato-based sauces in any metal container
- Do not use ceramics, enamel, and glass containers unless they are certified
- Do not put any item or container in a microwave unless it is certified for such use
- Do not use silicone cookware at temperatures above 220°C (428°F)

Additionally, the process one uses to cook a meal has a significant impact on the foods’ nutritional value.  Fortunately for everyone, basic cooking techniques can be used to prepare food in the healthiest way possible. Not surprisingly, these are also the techniques that best facilitateweight loss.

Unfortunately, one of the most popular food preparation techniques is deep frying. This technique requires cooking foods at high temperatures in fat.  The list of potential health issues associated with preparing foods in this manner is long.  Issues range from destruction of food nutrients, to adding unhealthy saturated fats and cholesterol, to exposure to cancer causing agents such as acrylamide and increased risks for weight gain and heart disease.  Do yourself a favor.  Stop eating deep-fried foods. There are more healthy preparation alternatives.

One popular alternative technique is grilling. It does not require that any type of oil be used, thus it avoids adding extra and unwanted fat.  While grilling is seen as a healthy alternative to frying, it should still be practiced with caution. There are certain side effects, such as an increased risk in developing colon and lung cancer, associated with eating grilled foods, especially in red meats. It’s important to observe safe grilling methods to minimize them. You should avoid eating burnt or charred meat, as they contain carcinogens that exacerbate cancer risks.

Boiling is also a widely recognized diet-friendly cooking technique. Similarly, it eliminates the use of oil in the cooking process, replacing it with water.

According to American author and physician Dr. Andrew Weil, MD, he prefers to steam and boil vegetables.  “I steam many of the vegetables I cook in my own kitchen, such as snow and snap peas, greens and artichokes. Steaming uses little energy, creates a minimum of mess and does the least damage to the nutrients in vegetables,” he said.

But when it comes to vegetables with thicker surfaces such as corn on the cob and potatoes, he tends to put them in the boiler. He warns, however, that “boiling tender vegetables will result in the loss of some of their nutrients in the cooking water.”

As far as healthy cooking is concerned, steaming is found to be the most ideal technique of all. According to a study published in the Journal of the Science of Food and Agriculture, steaming has the highest rate of nutrient retention in foods compared to other healthy methods.

An article titled Why Steaming Vegetables is Healthier Than Boiling by registered dietitian Janel Ovrut, MS RD has also confirmed this.

Posted on the My Family Doctor website, Ovrut believes that vegetables, in general, will be reduced of some of its naturalvitamins when it is boiled rather than steamed. “Many of the nutrients in vegetables can leach into the water when boiled. And the longer the vegetables are cooked, the more nutritional value is lost,” she explained.

Ovrut does offer a tip to those who favor boiling vegetables. “Get back those leached nutrients by using the water in a soup, sauce or stew,” she advised.

All the techniques listed above best seal the flavor and preserve the foods’ nutritional value, minus the excessive amounts of fat or salt commonly used to prepare typical American dishes. Delicious and satisfying meals without the guilt are always a perfect match for a healthy and well-balanced diet.
