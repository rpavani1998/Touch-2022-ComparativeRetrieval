Mike Bianchi: This is the reason Austin Rivers picked Duke over Gators

He beats UNC with shot heard 'round college basketball world

  • Comments
    16

(http://www.youtube.com/user/ESPN / February 9, 2012)



Mike Bianchi SPORTS COMMENTARY

10:46 p.m. EST, February 9, 2012

Now we know why Austin Rivers changed his mind and choseDuke over Florida.

It was because of moments like this.

Eternal moments.

Iconic moments.

Immortal moments.

Rivers, the Winter Park High grad and arguably the greatest prep basketball player in Orlando history, walked onto the court at the University ofNorth Carolina's Dean Dome Wednesday night as a Duke freshman.

He walked off as an ACC legend.

The son of former Magic and current Boston Celtics coach Doc Rivers, Austin scored 29 points and buried a buzzer-beating game-winning 3-pointer against the No. 5-ranked Tar Heels that already has etched his name in the annals of the hallowed Duke-North Carolina rivalry.

Let me put it in a way helmet-headed UF fans will understand: Rivers' deep trey over the outstretched arms of UNC 7-footerTyler Zeller is the basketball equivalent of Tim Tebow's jump pass against LSU.

The shot is already being talked about with the same reverence as great Duke-Carolina shots of yesteryear —Danny Ferry's 75-footer at the buzzer orJerry Stackhouse's dramatic dunk over Cherokee Parks or Chris Duhon's reverse layup. SportsCenter has replayed it over and over and from every angle. Your eyes are glued to the screen as the high, arcing ball drops through the hoop and your ears are assaulted as Dukie, er, Dickie Vee screams, "Oh! Oh! Unbelievable!"

It happened on college basketball's biggest platform and in college basketball's most storied rivalry. And this is exactly what Austin envisioned when he backed off his oral commitment to sign withBilly Donovan's Gators and instead signed with Coach K and Duke.

"I liked Florida, but you only get one chance to get it right," Austin told me last year, a few days after leading Winter Park High to its second consecutive big-school state championship. "There's no better stage than the Duke stage."

This is not an indictment of Donovan's program. In fact, you could even call it an endorsement of what Donovan has accomplished. The fact that Billy D. has built an elite college basketball program in a state where the sport is largely ignored is incredible. It is why I believe Donovan is the greatest coach — either football or basketball — in this state's history. Many coaches have won football national titles in this state, but only one has won a national title in basketball. And Donovan has not won just one; he has won two.

Yes, Donovan gets his share of top recruits, but nothing like the revolving door of McDonald's All-Americans on the rosters forMike Krzyzewski at Duke and Roy Williams at North Carolina. Donovan can coach with the best of the best, but one thing he will never be able to overcome is history and heritage.

College basketball just matters more at Duke than it does in Florida just as college football matters more at Florida than it does at Duke. It's nobody's fault; it's just the way fans were brought up.

In 30 years, old-timers along Tobacco Road will be puffing on their Marlboros and still be talking about Rivers' shot against North Carolina Wednesday night. Now let's rewind to the previous night when the Gators playedKentucky in Rupp Arena. If Rivers had made that same buzzer-beater to defeat the No. 1-ranked Wildcats and end their 48-game home winning streak, you know what Gators fans would be talking about today? They'd be talking about whether Will Muschamp can landMaryland prep product Stefon Diggs, the No. 2 wide receiver prospect in the country, according to Rivals.com

Football has always been king in theSEC, but never more than it is now. Back in the 1980s and '90s — when Charles Barkley,Shaq and others were roaming the hardwood — SEC basketball was much better across the board than it is today. There was actually a time in the 1990s whenAlabama had more players in theNBA than North Carolina and Duke.

With six straight national championships and counting, SEC football now casts a more enormous shadow than ever before. Not that SEC schools don't invest millions in their basketball programs, but all thatBCS money simply cannot buy passion, emotion and devotion.

Speaking selfishly, it would have been great for local sports fans (and writers) if Austin Rivers had decided to stay in-state to play college basketball, but it's now easy to see why he made the decision he did.

At UF, his shot would have been one of the best in Gators history.

At Duke, it's now one of the greatest in college basketball lore.
