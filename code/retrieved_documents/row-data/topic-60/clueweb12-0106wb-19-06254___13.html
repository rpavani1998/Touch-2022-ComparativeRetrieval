Memphis Tigers' switch to Big East football should win recruits, fan support

  • By Phil Stukenborg
  • Memphis Commercial Appeal
  • Posted February 8, 2012 at midnight, updated February 8, 2012 at 12:14 a.m.

ANALYSIS

Before news of a reported move to the Big East Conference surfaced Tuesday, the University of Memphis football program's future schedules -- games against Louisville and Miami in 2014 and Miami, Louisville, Missouri and Kansas in 2015 -- were what excited Tiger fans most.

Well, those games against teams from Bowl Championship Series conferences and the debut next fall of new coach Justin Fuente.

After Tuesday's approval by Big East presidents to offer an invitation to the U of M, the Tigers have another reason to celebrate. The program's long-awaited search for inclusion in one of the six major conferences is over, with the Tigers set to begin Big East play in 2013.

And what will that mean to a struggling football program, one that has dropped 31 of its past 36 games and hasn't had a winning season since 2007?

Fuente's second recruiting class should be better than his first. Average attendance, which has hovered around the 20,000 mark, should spike, as well. Annual payouts to Big East football members could approach $4 million in 2013, more than double what the U of M receives annually as a member of Conference USA, according to Boise State president Bob Kustra. Boise State joins the Big East in 2013 (football only).

Tuesday's news represents one of the most significant moments in Tiger football history, giving the U of M a better opportunity to compete with the Southeastern Conference for talent in the region. Memphis still won't be on equal footing with the SEC programs it recruits against, but would Ridgeway standout and Georgia signee Sheldon Dawson have honored his commitment to the U of M had the Tigers been in the Big East?

Memphis officials are remaining mum until today's noon news conference announcing the move. Others offered mixed reviews on what the Big East affiliation will mean to the football program.

''Now Justin Fuente will be able to walk into a kid's living room and say, 'We can compete for a national championship,''' said Mike Fredi, president of the Highland Hundred, the U of M's football booster group. ''He couldn't say that before.''

The Tigers will be joining a Big East that is losing Pittsburgh and Syracuse to the Atlantic Coast Conference and West Virginia to the Big 12. The league's footprint will stretch from California to Connecticut.

''This is not the Big East Memphis has dreamed of getting into for decades,'' saidDennis Dodd, national college football columnist for CBSSports.com. ''It is a marginal step up.''

Dodd said Big East football for 2013 -- Memphis, Cincinnati, Louisville, Houston, SMU, Central Florida, South Florida, Boise State, San Diego State, UConn and Rutgers -- will have a C-USA familiarity and lack marketability. Seven of the 11 (all but Boise, SDSU, Rutgers and UConn) are former C-USA members. Navy joins as a football-only member in 2015.

''I was told the addition of Pittsburgh and Syracuse alone to the ACC is worth more than what the new Big East will be worth in football,'' Dodd said. ''The theory is ESPN is not going to be interested because (the Big East) said no to them when they offered them $1 billion in their old form.''

A founding member of Conference USA in the mid-1990s, the U of M will benefit by having its basketball in arguably the nation's strongest conference. But despite a possibly de-valued television contract, the Big East will elevate U of M football by uniting it with perennial national power Boise State, along with solid programs at Cincinnati, Louisville and South Florida.

''This is only going to help Fuente,'' Fredi said.

There's the tricky matter of automatic qualifier status for college football's Bowl Championship Series too. Under the current BCS contract, each one of six conferences -- including the Big East -- received an automatic bid for its champion to play in one of the most lucrative bowls. With that comes a significant share of cash.

But many observers believe the automatic qualifier status will disappear after the current BCS contract expires in 2013, meaning that the long-coveted status as one of college football's big boys may lose much of its meaning by the time the Tigers join. It's unclear what the BCS' revenue structure would be in its new contract.
