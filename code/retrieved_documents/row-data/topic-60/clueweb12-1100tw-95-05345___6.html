Baylor's basketball, football teams share success

  • Comments
    0

Perry Jones III drives against Xavier in the Sweet 16. (Kevin C. Cox/Getty Images / March 24, 2012)

  • Style of play? To be determined
  • Printable 2012 NCAA tournament bracket

By Shannon Ryan, Tribune reporter

10:37 p.m. CDT, March 24, 2012

ATLANTA -- The Baylor men's basketball team watched some of its school's women's game in the locker room at the Georgia Dome, where it's preparing to face top-seeded Kentucky on Sunday in the NCAA tournament's South Region final. The women's team set the tone, beating Georgia Tech 83-68 in the regional semifinal in Iowa.

“The way our practice gym is set up and our whole facility there, we walk in and we see all of their trophies on the right,” forward Quincy Acy said. “Then we look on the left, and it's kind of empty. That's motivation.”

Baylor's athletic programs have seen a dramatic rise lately, especially in men's and women's basketball and football.

The men's players said they're tight with the female players and the many of the athletic teams at Baylor. QuarterbackRobert Griffin III, who won the Heisman Trophy, spent more time in the basketball locker room than many of the assistant coaches, basketball head coach Scott Drew said.

“It's a family environment,” guard Brady Haslip said. “Everybody at Baylor wants to see the other teams do well.”

Unfair criticism: Despite averaging 14 points and 7.7 rebounds, Baylor sophomore forwardPerry Jones III has faced criticism for not being more productive considering the hype he garnered entering college. Drew said that's unfair.

“He isn't a boastful person,” Drew said. “What's fair is theNBA project him high. The reason they project him high is because they know at 25, you've got an NBA All-Star potentially. Right now, Perry's not anywhere where he will be. He's a late bloomer in his game.”

Jones III scored 14 points to help the Bears beat Xavier in the Sweet 16.

Cold spell: Ohio State coach Thad Matta acknowledged Friday that William Buford's numbers in the two NCAA tournament games prior to Saturday's East Region final in Boston pointed to a slump.

The 6-foot-6 Buford, the only senior in Buckeyes' starting lineup, ranked third on the team in scoring with 14.4 points per game. He was 4-for-13 from the field and 2-for-7 from 3-point range for 13 points against Gonzaga. On Thursday against Cincinnati, he shot 1-for-8 from the field and finished with four points.

“The great thing about William is he usually bounces back, and hopefully the odds say (in the regional final) some higher percentage will be going in for him,” Matta said. “But yeah, we need Will to play well.”

Buford did just enough, finishing with 13 points on just 3-for-12 shooting in Ohio State's 77-70 victory overSyracuse.

Offensive spark: Syracuse guard Dion Waiters comes off the bench to average 12.7 points per game, second on the team. Entering Saturday, he led the Orange with 14.3 points per game in NCAA tournament games.

But Syracuse coach Jim Boeheim said the sophomore still has “lapses on the defensive end” that he needs to correct.

Waiters showed his offensive spark early in the first half Saturday. He completed a three-point play that sent Ohio State's Jared Sullinger to the bench with his second foul and followed with three more free throws later in the half.

Waiters finished with just nine points in 20 minutes as he fouled out in the final minute of the game.

Tribune reporter Colleen Kane contributed
