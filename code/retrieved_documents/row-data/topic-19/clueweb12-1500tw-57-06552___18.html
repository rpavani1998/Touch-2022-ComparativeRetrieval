Helpdesk Challenge – switching from PC to Mac

  • by Michael Gilson
  • in Computing,Laptops
  • 12 April 2012

53
comments

Most people I know that own a Mac tell me how much better they are than PCs, yet plenty of PC owners are hesitant about making the switch, or happily maintain that there are benefits to working with a PC.

There were a number of ad campaigns brought out by Apple both in America and in the UK, wittily highlighting the advantages of a Mac over a PC. Understandably, these reflected a corporate marketing message (the fun versus the staid and boring), and PCs hardly came off well.

There are plenty of defences for choosing a PC over a Mac, and for many people, these will be sufficient to deter them from switching over to the Apple side of the fence. As always, there are two sides to every argument.

Reasons to love a Mac

…the sleek and simplistic design looks great

…it works well with other Apple products

…you can run Windows alongside Mac OS X using Boot Camp

…there are fewer Mac-targeting viruses (although they do now exist)

…if you have any problems, it’s simple to call Applecare

…the software is slick and simple to use

Reasons to love a PC

…it’s fully customisable

…there’s a wide support system available

…there are stylish ultrabooks to choose from these days

…you can easily upgrade components

…there’s a wide range of software available

…you can easily find cheap laptop PCs

 

But how about those who have experienced both technologies, and have switched from PC to Mac? Or even – whisper it – switched from Mac to PC?

Have you switched? And if so, how long did it take to adapt, and what was the hardest part of the transition for you?

Lastly, can you share any advice with people who might be considering changing either from a Windows PC to a Mac or from a Mac to a PC?

  29. Evan
    Posted 13/04/2012

    Surely it’s horses for courses, and only one “type” of ser seems to have been represented. Now, I’m a PC user, always have been. However I A) appreciate the asthetics of Macs, B) appreciate the innovation, and C) appreciate how robust (both physically and in terms of operation) they are. When my pensioner dad asked what computer he should buy, I recommended a Mac. It’s operating system is easy to use, it “just works”, it looks nice in the house, and, most importantly, it doesn’t throw up lot’s of error messages which, like many old-school parents, he would read and try to understand. It’s ideal for him.

    But crucially, not for everyone.

    For a start, when he asked for advice he threw in the crucial line “money’s no object”. Let’s not forget I could pay £700 for a good Dell or Acer Laptop and the equivalent Macbook Pro would be around £1200. That’s not “a little more”. Equally, I appreciate the “you’ll make that back” arguement, but only if you need the services Apple require, and only if you make your Mac last. For example, my dad has had his for years and being a Mac it’s never broken, but equally he’s never had to upgrade it as he only uses it for internet, email, and Office stuff. You try and Upgrade a Mac and you’ll find that you ohave little scope to do it and it’s again, very expensive.

    Lastly, you’re locked in to Apple’s way of thinking. Mostly this is great, but not always. For example, even my dad suffered at it:- He bought my mum an iPod for christmas, they plugged it in, it said it wasn’t compatible with his version of iTunes, he should Download a newer version. he dowloaded it. iTunes said it wasn’t compatible with his version of OSX. He went to upload a newer version of OSX as they were both OSX 10 (10.44 to 10.5, I think), he couldn’t. He had to buy it for £70. Now you may say he should have checked, and yeah, maybe he should have, but it flies in the face of “Apples just work.” In my field of work, many of my colleagues use Final Cut Pro. It’s not my editor of choice but don’t get me wrong, it’s a brilliant programme that was even used to edit The Social Network. Well Apple have just “upgraded” it. They decided they wanted to aim at the amateur market, making the new programme all but useless to the professionals. The old version is still great, but within 18months, it’ll be dated useless. My colleagues are finding they have to spend about £2000 and huge amounts of time utterly re-learning how to do their job. Why have Apple done this? Who knows. Like I say, that’s Apple’s philosophy; we make decisions, like it or lump it. And because of the lack of competition, you’re stuck if you don’t like it.

    For me, the very things that are advantages to my dad, are disadvantages to me. I’m on a tighter budget and PCs aren’t “a bit cheaper”. In some cases a Mac equivalent is 100% more! The fact that the OS is so robust on a Mac actually makes it much more difficult to get into it and make it do what I want. I use lots of powerful software (I’m an editor), my PC is cheaper and easier to upgrade. If my dad’s computer breaks, apple picks it up for him, fixes it, sends it back free of charge. Brilliant! But it’s 5-10 working days, too long for me. When my PC breaks I can pop down my local computer store, get the parts, do it myself and be back up and running in a matter of hours. I need an awful lot of different programmes for an awful lot of tasks and PC offers a much wider variety and, because of that, better pricing.

    And I’ve not even started on using them for games. Many on Which won’t be interested, but that’s not the point really. PCs are so much better because they’re adaptable.

    Lastly, can I just say, the person that wrote “Our teenage rock band sons use them to make music. OK, you can do this on PC, but it’s better on Mac.” is talking absolute nonsense. The music professional’s choice, Pro Tools, is now designed and built on PCs. Up until about 5 years ago it was designed only for Macs, but because Apple annoyed the manufacturer (again with there “our way or the high-way” attitude) they moved to PC. Still available on Mac, but it’s now a conversion. Garage band is a great wee fun tool, yes, but simply saying “it’s better on Mac” is nonsense.

    So which is better? Neither! Ask yourself:
    - What’s your budget
    - What are you going to use it for
    - Will these tasks require your computer to be upgraded in the near future
    - Are you computer literate
    - If you are, do you like your computer running “just so” and don’t mind spending time doing that, or would you rather relinquish a bit control for solidity
    - Do you have any other devices you’d like to connect with. If so, what are they.

    Then get what you want. As long as you’re happy and it does what you want, you’ve bought the right one.
