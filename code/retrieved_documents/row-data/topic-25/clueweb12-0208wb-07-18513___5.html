NSS Labs: Internet Explorer 9 Offers Best Protection

  • By Neil J. Rubenking
  • December 14, 2010 12:43pm EST
  • Comments
Tweet

According to statistics from Trend Micro, at present 53 percent of malware attacks come directly from the Internet, compared with just 12 percent via e-mail. Many of those Internet-based attacks involve social engineering, tricking users into initiating or permitting a malicious download. In recognition of the prevalence of this threat, vector modernbrowsers include built-in filters to protect users. NSS Labs put six browsers to the test using real-world threats and found thatInternet Explorer 9 beta offers vastly more protection than the rest.

In 11 days of 24/7 testing, NSS Labs identified over 8,000 potential threats, whittled that list down to about 1,200 confirmed threats, and further culled the list to about 600 to ensure a representative mix. Internet Explorer 8 blocked an impressive 90 percent of the threats, an even better showing than in theprevious run of this test earlier in 2010.

Internet Explorer 9 achieved an outstanding 99 percent blocking rate, 5 percent of which was accomplished by the browser's new application reputation system. Roger Capriotti, group product manager for Internet Explorer, explained this technology. "Ninety percent of downloads involve a small set of files with established reputations," said Capriotti. "The remaining 10 percent is super interesting. Naturally it includes all the malware. In fact, 25 to 30 percent of these unknowns are later identified as malware. And fully 50 percent of them are new every day." The application reputation system identifies risky unknown downloads and advises the user to delete them.

Symantec's Norton Insight feature has a similar function. Put simply, it identifies files that are not known to Symantec, that are very new, and that are very uncommon as dangerous. However, Rick Moy, president of NSS Labs, said that his tests haven't shown Symantec's technology to be as effective.

Microsoft's Capriotti said this built-in protection against malicious sites is just one component of the coming Internet Explorer 9 experience. "We're very focused on the best browsing experience for billions of Windows customers," said Capriotti. "For Internet Explorer 9 beta we've built a fast browser with hardware acceleration We completely rewrote the Javascript engine so it's the fastest. And we offer a new, clean streamlined user interface, with a more interactive and immersive experience."

The other browsers in the test, Chrome, Firefox, Opera, and Safari, scored much lower than Internet Explorer. They also uniformly scored lower than their own performance in the previous round of testing. Firefox detected 19 percent of the threats, down from 29 percent. Safari detected 11 percent and Chrome detected 6 percent. Opera turned in a perfect zero; it did not detect any of the socially engineered malware samples.

The same technology that blocks malware sites also protects against fraudulent (phishing) sites.PCMag's antiphishing testing uses Internet Explorer as a touchstone. Surprisingly, the majority of third-party antiphishing products can't do better than the browser's built-in protection. In past years we also used Firefox for comparison, but its phishing detection rate declined to the point where it was no longer useful. Jeb Haber, principal program manager lead for Internet Explorer security, did point out that the number of malware-hosting sites is vastly greater than the number of active phishing sites.

Chrome, Firefox, and Safari all rely on Google's Safe Browsing service to identify malicious sites. Curiously, NSS Labs found that despite using the same service, the three products had significantly different overall detection rates. Safari in particular had trouble communicating with the service, which partially explains the differences. For full details on the results of this test visit theNSS Labs web site.
