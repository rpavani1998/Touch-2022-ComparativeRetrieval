Cooking with gas: It's a blast!

Posted: July 28, 2011 - 4:05am  |   Updated: July 28, 2011 - 2:30pm

OCTAVIO JONES/The Times-Union
Florida State College culinary students Kevin Lecointe (left) and Racheal Davis observe their instructor Joe Harrold cook chicken on a gas stove at the school’s North Campus. Harrold said many of his students have never cooked with gas before starting the program, but soon come to love the predictability of gas. Jack Jacksonville on Facebook





By Dan Macdonald jacksonville.com Copyright 2011 The Florida Times-Union. All rights reserved. This material may not be published, broadcast, rewritten or redistributed.
July 28, 2011 - 12:05am Cooking with gas: It's a blast!

"Now you're cooking with gas." That was a popular phrase used by the cool cats back in the day. For the past several years, I've been cooking with gas at home and I love it.

In 2007, we remodeled our kitchen. The project started when we investigated installing a better exhaust fan, and as most things go in my house, before I knew it Judy had an entire kitchen remodel under way. I laugh about it now, but I can hardly remember cooking without my gas oven top.

While I love gas, it scares me to death.

I'll be comfy in bed, as cozy as I will be all night and I'll have to get out from under the covers to check one more time that I turned off the gas burners. I'll return to bed with a relieved mind, but my back never returns to that perfect position.

Part of this comes from my Western New York/Pennsylvania upbringing. During college, I lived with my grandmother in an aged house that lacked proper insulation. Besides the old furnace in the basement, we had smaller gas stoves in the other rooms, especially in the upstairs apartment where I lived. When the winter winds blew hard and in the right direction, it would blow out the flames from some of jets, leaving them exposed to ooze unignited natural gas into the apartment's entryway. And people wonder why I am a light sleeper.

While I'm not crazy about heating with gas, I've actually cooked on it many times in my life. I learned to make my Uncle Don's wonderful scrambled eggs on his gas stove. My first bit of adolescent responsibility was when he showed me at a young age how to light the burners with a match. His old gas stove top didn't have a pilot light.

Later, I had two apartments in Jacksonville with gas stoves. They came with pilot lights but I told the supers at both places not to ignite them. "I don't cook much. I won't need the oven. I'll just light the cook top with a match when I need it."

I didn't trust that trickling flame to stay alive in the middle of a cold windy night.

Today, my gas stove top ignites with an electric spark. No pilot light needed. I bake and roast in the electric convection oven. It's the best of both worlds.

So, why is gas better than electric? I'll let a pro tell you:

"Let me put it to you in one word: control," said Joe Harrold, professor of Culinary Arts at Florida State College at Jacksonville's North Campus. "With gas, you can control the heat. When you shut it down, it is shut down. With electric, the burner is still hot for another 20 minutes."

Harrold said many of his students have never cooked with gas prior to enrolling but soon love its predictability. As for my fear of pilot lights, he said my respect for them is justified, and it is part of the curriculum.

"We teach them how to light it when it goes out. You can't just light a match and walk away. That's dangerous," he said.

It's dangerous for the obvious reason that you don't want gas filling a kitchen. But it's also a threat to job security to the rookie line cook who thinks he has heated the stoves in preparation for a day of cooking and upon his return finds them cold when he needs to use them.

Cooking on a gas stove top is like playing a musical instrument. I can adjust the flame to the proper height. I can move the pot off the flame so just a portion of it is being kissed by the flame if I want to simmer something. It makes timing a meal so much easier.

Now, there are drawbacks to gas. My old electric stove was a dragon. Boiling water on those hot coils took no time. I was afraid my gas stove was broken the first time boiled water on it. It seemed to take twice as long to come to a boil. Now when I get home, I fill a pot with water and let it boil while I change clothes and check the mail. By the time I'm ready to cook, so is the water.

The next time I buy a gas stove (which I hope isn't for a while, but I covet stoves like some men do cars), I would want one with more high Btu burners. I have one larger burner and a medium one and two smaller ones. By moving pots around and positioning them not directly over the flame, I can still do slow cooking over the high Btu flame when necessary.

Even with these drawbacks, I still prefer gas.

There are new ceramic cook tops, but I am afraid of scratching them, as I can get a little rough and clumsy when I have four pots going at once. The new magnetic surfaces are space age, but I don't want to have to buy special pots.

Just give me fire on metal - cast iron preferably - and I am a happy guy.

We decided to install gas because it didn't add a lot of extra expense to the remodeling job. Our stove backs against an exterior wall. The gas company simply drilled a hole in the side of the house and ran the gas line straight to the stove.

Because our kitchen is near the patio, I also had them run a line to the backyard so I could hook my gas grill to the same huge tank. Another fantastic advantage - I've never run out of gas while grilling.

I used to have two small propane tanks when I grilled. The spare was always filled. Well, almost always; there was the time on my sister-in-law's birthday when I ran out in the middle of cooking and found myself with two empty tanks. Good thing the kid at the hardware store didn't have a date that night, because he was filling my tank after closing time. I still try to do business at that store because of that service.

Changing gas bottles on a hot grill is also dangerous. Once, while my father was grilling steak, the bottle ran dry. Being a good son, I volunteered to change it for him. Of course, the empty bottle didn't come loose easily, and when I gave it a jerk to get it off the grill, the back of my hand slapped the hot grill bottom. The burn wasn't bad, but to this day Dad feels bad about that household accident. Don't worry about it, Pa, as I remember you generously treated it with some Scotch - you poured it in me, not on the burn.
