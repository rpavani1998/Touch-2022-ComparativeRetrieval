Diesel models becoming more desirable

 

  By Scott Deveau, Financial Post February 27, 2012
   
  • Story
  • Photos ( 1 )



 
 

Audi Q7 TDI instrument panel, showing an instant fuel economy of 5.9L/100km at 70 mph on a US Interstate where the speed limit is 70 mph.

Photograph by: Derek McNaughton , Canwest News Service

The state of California recently announced plans to offer owners of new low-emission Chevrolet Volts a US$1,500 tax credit on top of the US$7,500 rebate already given to those who buy the electric vehicle.

The cherry on top? Lawmakers decided to let owners of the new Volt drive it solo in California's carpool lanes.

It was just the latest example of how governments across North America have been pushing consumers toward electric and hybrid vehicles and off fossil fuels.

But to little effect. In Canada last year, hybrid and electric vehicles amounted to just 1.5% of the total vehicles sold, with sales weighed down by consumer anxiety around the technology and price. A recent KPMG study forecast that electrified vehicles were unlikely to make up more than 15% of the overall market in North American until at least 2025.

This has automakers searching for more consumer-friendly alternatives as they struggle to meet the increasingly stringent efficiency standards set in the U.S. and elsewhere.

Oddly enough, that has brought companies like electricity booster General Motors Co. back to diesels in their efforts to curb emissions.

GM said this week it would bring a diesel variation of its popular compact car, the Cruze, to North America next year. It is perhaps the best sign that diesels are entering the mainstream on this continent.

"It's all centred around fuel economy," says Matt Crossley, GM Canada's director of engineering. "Hybridization and electrification is part of it.

"Diesel is part of it. There's probably going to be other fuels in here, like natural gas, that are likely going to be a part of it."

Diesel engines have, however, presented themselves as one of the more proven technologies in the marketplace. They have long made up nearly half of the market in Europe and elsewhere.

But their legacy of being loud, sulphur-spewing beasts clogging North American highways has been something that has weighed on sales here. As a result, the market for diesel engines in North America has been primarily reserved for trucks because of their better torque, and to European imports.

But major technological advancements over the past few decades, as well as new regulations on the fuel that fires them here, has breathed new life into the diesel market in North America.

"The modern diesels are just fabulous engines," says David Cole of the Center for Automotive Research. "A lot of the historic problems of noise and smell are pretty much gone, and diesel fuel is more widely available."

The value proposition is clear: Diesel engines offer similar fuel savings to hybrids at a lower cost and without the anxiety attached to a new technology. As Sergio Marchionne, Chrysler chief executive, put it at the Detroit Auto Show this year: "We think that the market is ready to listen to a diesel story."

Chrysler will also be bringing a diesel version of its Grand Cherokee to market in the second quarter of 2013.

It's not just the U.S. automakers joining the party.

Japan's Mazda Motor Corp. also plans to introduce diesel variations of several of its popular vehicles next year in North America, although it has yet to officially say which models.

"It's part of our environmental strategy. A lot of other manufacturers are looking at hybrids and electric vehicles," says Sandra Lemaitre, Mazda Canada spokeswoman. "We're introducing diesel to bring down the average fuel economy for our vehicle fleet."

Diesel engines on average offer about 25% greater fuel efficiency than a traditional combustion engine, or roughly the same amount as a hybrid, says George Magliano, IHS Global Insight analyst. But the extra value comes in the selling price. The average diesel-powered vehicle will cost about $2,000 more than a traditional internal-combustion engine, while a hybrid variation might cost upward of $7,000 more, depending on the make and model, he says.

"I think the bloom is off the rose on electrification a little bit and people are looking at the traditional internal combustion engine and the diesel engine as a way of hitting those fuel-economy standards," he says. "Diesel used to be 'd' for 'dirty.' That's not the case anymore."

But Mr. Magliano says there would have to be more options in the North America marketplace before consumers, in particular in the U.S., really buy into diesel.

Diesel sales accounted for about 1% of total U.S. sales in 2011, while the hybrid market was more than double that.

The opposite is true, however, in Canada, where consumers are more efficiency oriented. Diesel sales made up about 3% of sales in Canada last year, compared to about 1.5% for hybrid and all-electric vehicles, says Dennis DesRosiers, a leading auto analyst in the country.

Mr. DesRosiers says he believes the somewhat tepid response to electric and hybrid vehicles in Canada is a result of the compromises consumers have to make in order to drive them.

"It might be size, handling, power, trunk space, design or other things, and at the same time pay a lot more," he says. "With diesel, you also pay more, but you do not have to compromise."

Josh Bailey, vice-president of research at Canadian Black Book, said diesel engines also carry the added advantage of a higher resale value.

For example, the diesel option on the older Grand Cherokee that went out of production in 2008 cost consumers roughly $6,000 extra. But the resale value of the diesel Grand Cherokee commands up to a $10,000 premium in the secondary market relative to the traditional gasoline version, he said.

With the fuel savings and higher resale value, buyers of new diesel-powered cars can find themselves with an extra couple of thousand dollars in their pocket after just a few years.

At the same time, hybrids in the used market seldom come close to making up the original amount paid, he said.

"It speaks volumes about what people want to buy," Mr. Bailey says. "Where diesels are offered, we definitely see that it commands a premium [in the used market] over the gas variant, and often times, it is a premium over what the feature originally sold for."

It is perhaps not surprising then that Peter Hatges, a partner at KPMG LLP and leader of its automotive group, expects the uptake in diesel engines to greatly outpace hybrids in the coming years.

He says he expects diesel sales to make up about 15% of the market in Canada within the next eight years, while it will likely be at least 15 years before hybrids and electrics hit that point.

Part of the discrepancy is that the diesel engine is a proven technology, whereas there is still a lot anxiety about electrified vehicles' batteries, which can cost upward of $10,000 each.

"Diesel technology is not new. It's new to North America in the sense that it has been improved so much.- the engines themselves are very robust," Mr. Hatges says.

Volkswagen estimates that about 40% of its Canadian customers will choose a diesel variation when given the choice between it and a traditional gasoline engine. The gap narrows substantially in higher-end vehicles, where the $2,000 premium is negligible.

Mercedes-Benz estimates that between 75% and 85% of its customers will choose diesel when given both options.

But Thomas Tetzlaff, VW Canada spokesman, notes that diesel sales are made without the aid of government subsidies. About 30% of VW's overall sales in Canada are diesel engines compared to 50% in Europe, where consumers are rewarded for driving more efficient vehicles of all types through the tax regime there.

In North America the push has been primarily focused on rewarding consumers of electric or hybrid vehicles, he said.

"In Europe, there is very significant government support of diesel cars," he said. "If the government wants to make efficiency part of the course of day-to-day business activity and part of the buyer's decision, well then, efficiency of any sort should be rewarded."
