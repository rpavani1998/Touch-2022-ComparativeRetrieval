A Vision of the Future - Available Now - New player, Banke Accessory Drives launches 'Plug-in' Hybrid system



The 26tonne gross weight Volvo/Zoeller refuse collection vehicle (RCV) fitted with Banke modular 'Plug-in' battery pack was demonstrated to members of the Danish Parliament in early January

This story has it all - an idealistic inventor, the need to save the planet and a sceptical 'public'. Malcolm Bates reports from Copenhagen on a small Danish manufacturer of a 'plug-in' hybrid drive bin lift and compaction system, and how it's benefiting from a whole new way of doing business - the Lean Energy Cluster.

To be honest, Rasmus Banke doesn't look like a 'mad professor'. In fact, my hair is wilder than his. And I'm probably closer to being 'mad' than he is too. But that should just illustrate how appearances can be deceptive. And why the views Rasmus Banke holds could be key to our future.


A New Year, a New Approach to business? A well-attended meeting was held inside the Parliament building by the Lean Energy Cluster to outline the advantages of companies working together (in a 'cluster') on a specific project

I've been invited to a special meeting in the Danish Parliament. It has been convened by an organisation called The Lean Energy Cluster (of which Banke is a member) to discuss 'the future'. The future of our planet that is. The future role Denmark should play (as a small nation with a big reputation for turning visionary ideas into workable government policies) and the ways in which business might be done in the future, in a resource starved world. It also involves the future of the humble garbage truck - and the role it could play in helping us maximise what resources we have left. As I'm sure you'll agree, that's a pretty demanding agenda for a single meeting - so let's get down to analysing the key elements....

None of us know what life on earth will be like in 50 years time. And as it involves forces outside of 'waste management', let's put that to one side. How about Denmark? This small, but incredibly honest democratic European state has an enviable record on sustainability, on implementing visionary environmental policies and being prepared to invest - by which I mean 'spend' citizens' tax money - to a far greater degree than just about any other country on earth. Why do they do it? Partly because Danes think it's the right thing to do, but also as Martin Lidegaard, the Danish minister for energy explained, because investing in long-term solutions really can pay a good return while improving the environment and creating more jobs.

In fact, designing and building high tech environmental solutions is now very much the 'industry' Denmark would like to be known for internationally. And unlike many other democracies, all the political parties in Denmark support this high investment approach.

Save energy - boost economic growth

What difference can individual Governments make to a global problem?

The long-sighted policies of Denmark should serve as an example to us all - and shame those larger national governments that claim the imposition of strict new environmental policies will damage growth and cause increased unemployment. The example set by Denmark explodes that myth - environmental technologies look set to create over 10,000 new jobs in the next five years. "But when it comes to more specific policies like reducing energy consumption by the country as a whole, and by organisations and individuals in particular, the Danish Government strongly believes all Government departments, regional and local city councils and communes, should be leading by example," the Minister confirmed to Waste Management World.


The Zoeller-produced compactor body/hopper and binlifters can be operated without need to run the engine - demonstrating how noise and fuel can be saved

Even to the point of, for example, waste management authorities being encouraged to invest in more advanced technical solutions, rather than - as currently - be pressured to accept the lowest price for new vehicles, or when awarding an operational contract? "Yes, very much so. The Danish Government believes such targeted investment is vital if energy consumption is to be reduced and our economy is to grow in a future of increasing energy costs," he adds. 'Green energy' exports totalled over 50 billion Danish Krona ($8.5 billion) in 2011. Almost 10% of all exports.

And if you think Denmark has a pretty positive record on recycling ratios and minimising landfill - as it has - then that's as nothing compared to the impressive record the county has on energy conservation. Thanks to previous long-term government policies and investment, Denmark uses no more energy today than it did in 1980, while also achieving an impressive reduction in CO2 emissions well above the European average. Over that same period, economic growth has increased four-fold.

But because we don't tend to think of Denmark as a major industrial producer of manufactured goods, it's interesting to note that the Danish Government uses its remit to help encourage new, achievable ways of creating new technologies to help boost an already impressive record on creating what we might term an environmental industry sector. How? This is where 'The Lean Energy Cluster' comes in.

If you come from a time when the only trends in industry seemed to involve large, multinational organisations and venture capitalists mopping-up smaller innovative companies, then this story will come - excuse the environmental pun - as a breath of fresh air. What is a 'Cluster'? It's a new way of doing business. Forget one company needing to 'be in charge', or owning all the other players, a cluster is lots of smaller specialists coming together on a specific project and co-operating together as equals.

Significant savings

It works like this: a specific project is identified as the right thing to do environmentally. In this case we're talking about replacing the hydraulically-driven compaction equipment and bin/container lifters on a refuse collection vehicle (garbage truck), by a 'Plug-in' battery/electric system. Why is this such a good idea?

The most publicised gain is in a reduction of CO2 (typically 15 tonnes per vehicle per year) and a reduction in diesel fuel consumption of 5000 litres per vehicle per year - both significant figures. But I'd suggest the other key gain - a considerable reduction in operational noise - has not been promoted as widely as it could have been.

There is some nervousness amongst some RCV manufacturers because as we discovered in the previous issue of Waste Management World, if more operators purchased large numbers of quieter diesel electric hybrid RCVs and double shifted them with two crews during each 24 hour period, which helps recoup the added purchase cost for obvious reasons, this might result in fewer number of vehicles being needed in total.

Banke sees things rather differently. Firstly, after a background in energy systems, he is convinced that the electrical power take-off (PTO) is more efficient than the traditional hydraulic PTO. Fuel consumption is less because the truck power unit is only required to propel the vehicle along and not to generate hydraulic power while stationary.

Most observers would accept that the constant rise and fall of engine revs, as hydraulic binlifters are used, is what makes night time waste collection operations in residential areas unacceptable. In contrast, a 'plug-in' battery powered auxiliary drive enables the truck's engine to be switched off while loading and packing, or stay at a constant tick-over. So far, so environmentally friendly.

A new way forward

While a number of manufacturers are now offering such 'hybrid' options, or are involved in trying to build prototypes, according to Banke, there is an overriding problem.

"Designing and building systems using the latest battery technology is a very specialised business and we should ask why each manufacturer needs an in-house solution? In other branches of industry such as building construction, shipping, industrial processes and power generation for example, there are large specialists such as Danfoss that research, design and build systems for incorporation into wider projects. I would argue that in helping to promote a new generation of refuse collection trucks, the waste and recycling sector should follow that model," he suggests.

Banke is passionate about these issues. So passionate in fact, he gave up a top job with Danfoss A/S in order to found Banke Accessory Drives. Already, the company is working with ten or more 'partners' on this first project. And when you hear that from initial concept, to starting the company, putting together the Cluster, to actually having a prototype Hybrid RCV ready to enter service took just two years, you might be impressed enough to conclude that inventor or not, Rasmus Banke is far from 'mad'. The Lean Energy Cluster has direct support from the Danish Government, the local regional Councils and the leading technical university of Southern Denmark. It also has the support of Manfred Zoeller, CEO of leading German manufacturer Zoeller Waste Systems and Meldgaard, one of Denmark's leading commercial waste contractors.

So what does all this mean? It will hopefully mean that the concept of more efficient, environmentally friendly refuse collection vehicles is no longer a dream, unique to one brand, or another. As Soren Jessing, development engineer for Banke Accessory Drives explains: "Our solution is modular. It's adaptable without a major increase in tooling costs - so will benefit from an economy of scale."

Could a hybrid option help win contracts?

We'll soon find out if he's right - with a fleet of over 150 'conventional' diesel/hydraulic PTO-powered refuse collection and recycling trucks, Meldgaard is keen to convert to hybrids as soon as possible. Why? "We are putting the Banke Hybrid to work alongside our existing vehicles so we can compare performance," CEO Henrik Meldgaard, explained. "We were very interested in the model adopted by the Commune in the city of Solna in Sweden (as reported in Waste Management World November/December 2011).


The hybrid RCV will enter service with Danish waste management operator Meldgaard, to provide comparisons with existing diesel-fuelled hydraulically activated RCVs

Putting a value against the environmental gains in a waste collection contract as a way of encouraging higher standards appeals to us," he adds. "And we are already talking to several Communes (Councils) with which we have contracts on that basis." Meldgaard has sixteen waste contracts in Denmark and is looking to win more - a 'hybrid option' may just provide the way to win them.


This view shows how little body space is taken up by the battery pack. Located by just eight bolts, the same basic 'module' can be designed to fit any chassis/body combination

Adding environmental 'House Points' to the contract bidder prepared to invest in more expensive, environmentally friendly vehicles (as opposed to just the cheapest) is supported by the Danish Government and regional governments such as Region Syddanmark - another active supporter of the Lean Energy Cluster. But aside from a radically different business model, are there any technical issues that need taking on board? Banke and Soren Jessing are adamant that a 40 kWh (effective)/700 volt battery pack is essential for this application, as opposed to using a 96 volt option.

Research shows battery pack life of more than 2500 cycles using LiFePo4 battery technology. The Banke system uses a 400 volt PM (permanent magnet) motor with variable RPM control - which is 60% lighter than some competitive designs.

The system has enough power to lift up to 1200 containers per battery pack charge - all within a modular package just 456 mm long, 2500 mm wide and 2000 mm high. This 'pod' fits between truck can and compaction body of most waste collection vehicles with minimal loss of body length and an increase in unladen weight of just 950 kg. The batteries are designed to be chargeable (by a simple domestic plug) within four hours - useful when using cheaper off-peak power and essential if a double shift pattern is to be used in future. The charger is built-in.

And? Another interesting thing about The Lean Energy Cluster approach is that it lends itself to new partners being added, together with local assembly within the growing economies - thus creating new jobs and saving energy in waste collection and recycling on a global basis.
