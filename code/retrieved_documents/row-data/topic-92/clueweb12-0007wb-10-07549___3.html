Mulching

From Ekopedia

Jump to: navigation, search


Mulching consists in laying an organic cover around plants (usuallystraw). This technique has many advantages for the gardener as well as for the environment. The main ones being the ground remains moist as it prevents evaporation and saves frequent watering, this means less water wasted but also lessweeds that would hinder germination.

[edit] Advantages

Laying organic wastes as mulch enables the gardener to recycle them. The mulch will decay and rot, providing humus to the soil ; thanks to these natural nutrients, chemical ones are useless so it's also a means to contribute to the preservation of microfauna.

Besides being environment friendly, this technique also has advantages for the gardener who saves time : no need to weed, hoe or till, thus less soil erosion. It also saves money as less water is needed, no fertilizers and no need to take organicwastes to the local dump.

[edit] Setting

Before setting the mulch, the soil should be hoed about 5 inches deep. The mulch can be set any time of the year except in Winter, this can be done manually or using a trowel for tiny patches so as to lay an even cover about 4 to 5 inches thick. It's better not to form little heaps around the plants to avoid an excess of moisture.

[edit] Materials

Many vegetal species can be used as mulch but the cover can also be composed of synthetic material. It's always better to use local species rather than imported materials from tropical plants such as cacao pods which are much more expensive. Components derived from conifers provoke acidification, so they're not suitable for all plants but are good for heath plants.

[edit] Double mulch

Double mulch combines the benefits of two complementary mulches, e.g. dead leaves create humus but will easily be blown by the wind so covering these with homecompost will protect this first layer.

[edit] Ramial Chipped Wood (RCW)


perennials covered with RCW.

Ramial Chipped Wood (RCW) is the name given to chipped branches of deciduous trees of less than 3 inches in diameter, pinales can be mixed, up to 20%. These branches or very young trees that compose RCW are the richest part of the tree, they contain 75% of the minerals, amino acids, proteins and catalysts. By extension, RCW also refers to innovative agricultural methods, the use of chipped wood as an upper layer or as mulch will recreate a "forest floor". RCW helps pedogenesis which is necessary to the formation of humus. The use of RCW is considered as essential for organic farming and sustainable farming and to fight desertification.
