Mira Sorvino: Diabetes Is a Family Affair By Gina Roberts-Grey, Special to Lifescript

When Oscar-winning actress Mira Sorvino learned her father had type 2 diabetes, she took quick action. In this Lifescript exclusive, find out how she helped improve her dad’s health and lowered her children’s risk of the chronic disease...

Acting is a family business for Paul and daughter Mira Sorvino. So when Paul, 71, a veteran character actor who plays tough guys, was diagnosed with type 2 diabetes, it was only natural for Mira, 43, to make this a family affair too.

The role of caretaker came easily to Mira, who won an Academy Award for best supporting actress in “Mighty Aphrodite.”

“After my dad was diagnosed, I realized I had to educate myself about his condition,” says Sorvino, who also charmed moviegoers in “Romy and Michele’s High School Reunion.” “I wanted to be equipped with the right tools to help keep him on track with his treatment.”

That’s not easy with a dad who enjoys hearty Italian meals and produces pasta sauces for supermarkets.

Like many diabetics, Paul initially controlled blood-sugar levels by lowering carbohydrate and sugar intake, exercising, losing weight and taking prescription medicine.

At first, the day-to-day maintenance overwhelmed him, and he kept his diagnosis a secret. He didn’t want to “burden” Mira and her siblings, the actress says. Plus, the former “Law & Order” cop was used to playing it tough.

That’s why the father-daughter duo launched the “Diabetes Co-Stars” awareness program, which aims to encourage relatives and friends to support those with the disease. It afflicts nearly 24 million Americans, including 11.5 million women. In this Lifescript exclusive, Mira Sorvino reveals what she’s doing to keep her pasta-loving father healthy and cut the odds that she – or her three kids – will develop diabetes.

What were your father’s symptoms before his diagnosis?
He was very tired, thirsty and had a general malaise. No one knew these were symptoms [of diabetes] though. He ignored them for quite a while.

How did he find out?
He saw his doctor for something unrelated and the physician took a blood test that showed his blood sugar was in the 420s. [Normally, this level should be between 70-150.]

What did he do after finding out?
He actually kept the diagnosis secret for a few months. He was in denial. He didn’t completely follow his doctor’s advice, but didn’t completely ignore it either.

How did you discover he was diabetic?
It was quite frightening. I was at my father’s house recuperating from having a new baby and trying to avoid the flu that the rest of my family had. [Mira is married to actor Christopher Backus, and they have three children: Mattea, 6; Johnny, 4; and Holden, 1.]

Dad is a wonderful cook and had made this delicious pastafagioli, a traditional Italian dish with pasta and beans. About 20 minutes after he enjoyed a generous helping, he turned a purple-ashen color, looked down and slumped forward in his chair. What did you do?
I was instantly worried. I asked him, “What’s going on?” and he said he didn’t know. I thought to call an ambulance, but he just wanted to lie down in his room.

I stood vigil outside the door for about 20 minutes, repeatedly asking, “Are you OK?” Finally, he said he was and told me: “I think it was the diabetes. I took pills, and I think it’s getting better.”

Why had he kept it from you?
He’s from a generation where men were tough guys brought up to be John Wayne.

Ironically, I don’t think of this knowledge as a burden. I want to support and encourage him to take care of himself.

Is there a family history of diabetes?
My father’s brother has it. But I think you fail to consider that it can actually happen to you, or your loved one, until a confirmed diagnosis.

After that health scare, what went through your head?
I realized diabetes was serious, yet I had no knowledge about it or how to make sure my dad was safe and healthy. That’s why I wanted to become involved in his health. I know family and friends are often clueless about what diabetes is and how to help.

What are you doing to support your father?
When his original treatment wasn’t working, I encouraged him to talk to the doctor and try new medicines.

I also try to get him to eat more healthfully, which is tough on him. I used to stock the freezer with ice cream to accompany cake. Now it [holds] sugar-free, low-fat options. I serve him huge salads, not big bowls of pasta or fried foods. I encourage him to exercise more too. He has always played tennis, but I urge him to do more than that. Now when he’s at my house, we walk on the beach.

Has your father made other dietary changes?
He doesn’t eat sugar anymore, which was a big change. Even more significant is that he cooks low-carb pasta and eats less of it than before.

Being Italian and eating pasta is important for our family; it’s a way of life. My grandfather was born in Naples and had pasta once a day – sometimes 2-3 times a day. When I was growing up, he wouldn’t come to our house for Thanksgiving unless there was a pasta dish too.

So cutting back and going low-carb was a very big step for my dad. To make dietary changes easier for diabetics who enjoy pasta, he hopes to produce a low-carb version with his company [Paul Sorvino Foods].

Is his diabetes under control now?
Thanks to diet, exercise and medicine, he’s doing great. He actually looks younger than he did a few years ago. Like my uncle, who also has diabetes and lost a lot of weight to [treat] his diabetes and [boost] cardiac health, my father is improving his health.

The way he has handled this [diagnosis] is brave and admirable. At first, though, he was afraid people would regard him as feeble.

How has your dad’s diagnosis spurred you to make changes to ward off diabetes?
I’ve always led a healthy lifestyle and been active, so it didn’t spark many lifestyle changes. In college, I was on the crew team. I love to run -- it’s my favorite exercise. And I always work out because being in shape is part of my job description. But I made some significant dietary changes.

Like what?
 We were eating too much sugar.

I always compensated by burning it off through exercising, but seeing my father and uncle live with diabetes made me realize that wasn’t enough. I needed to modify my sugar intake and set a healthy example for my kids.

How tough was that?
Sugar is everywhere you turn… ice cream and cupcake stores. I’m trying to switch the kids to eating more fruits and yogurt versus those sugary things.

We don’t drink sugar soda. I don’t eat a lot of pasta and pizza either, but I overdose on breads and sweets. Now I realize the importance of cutting down on those things.

What are you teaching your kids about diabetes?
First, they see what we’re doing for Grandpa and know he can’t eat certain things. They’re starting to understand some foods might not be healthy.

So we’re cutting out some unhealthy foods like macaroni and cheese when we eat out. Instead, I’ll make a healthier version at home by mixing eggs into hot pasta and use the heat of pasta to cook the egg with a tiny bit of low-fat cheddar and butter.

They also understand that taking walks on the beach and playing tennis with Grandpa keeps their bodies healthy. What have you learned from your father’s diabetes?
We’ve all learned that you can be a happy, healthy person despite diabetes – that you can be empowered by taking control over your blood sugar levels.

Diabetes is something that affects the whole family, not just the person diagnosed with it. We’ve made [managing it together] a positive in all our lives.

How Much Do You Know About Diabetes?
In the United States alone, 23.6 million people have diabetes. And 5.6 million of them don’t even know it. Unfortunately, misinformation about diabetes is rampant – and mixing up the facts about this disease can have dire consequences. Are you confused about diabetes?
