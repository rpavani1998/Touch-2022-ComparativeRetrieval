I couldn't tell you how well that particular stove works.  The idea (a wax-powered stove) seems sound anyway.  If you like the idea, might give it a shot.  Obviously, try it out at home first.

Don't be too impressed by their claims of "turning the wax into gas" stuff.  Candles do that.  Wax melts, then evaporates due to the flame heat, and it's the gas that burns.  That's why a candle burns only at the wick and above (where the "gas" is).  If the liquid or solid burned, the whole candle would light up at once.  The "our stove burns wax gas!" line is just a gimmick to make it sound fancy.  They seem to have helped the flame a bit (kinda like an alchy stove helps project and intensify the flames), but burning candle wax in gas form is hardly new.

That said, we all know wax burns, and enough of it burning at once burns plenty hot, so I don't see why it couldn't be a stove.  Again, try it out in advance to see how long it takes, how much fuel it consumes for a meal, etc... and best of luck.

Snowfox, if you're wanting to hang around on internet forums, I'd personally suggest not taking much of anything personally.  It's just the way things are on the 'net.  Don't sweat it.  THAT said, it really is a valuable place to get feedback, especially about gear.

As you can tell so far, not many folks have used this stove in particular, myself included, so all the opinions are hypothetical.  If you're convinced it'd work for you, try it out... if you use it and write an impartial field review, it'd obviously be valuable!  I'd read it.

Until then, consider each of the concerns people have about the stove and ask yourself how valid they may (or may not) be.  For instance, the concerns on Whiteblaze about it not burning cleanly (and leaving a bit of soot on the pot) would not bother me one bit.  I use a wood stove on long trips--multiple weeks at a time in SE Alaska--and the soot is of little concern to me.  In my opinion how well this wax stove will work in the dead of winter depends what you want to use it for.  If you'll always have access to liquid water and plan to just cook a small meal a couple times a day, honestly it'd probably do ya' just fine.  However, if you need to melt snow for all your water (1-2 gallons a day), you really need something that can put out a ton of BTU's, and I'm not sure that stove would do the job.  The little yellow flame just doesn't seem that strong.  You wouldn't want to spend multiple hours each morning waiting for that thing to warm up a gallon pot of snow, especially if you need to get moving.  In my experience it takes a long time to melt that much snow even with the maximum blast of a white-gas stove on a roaring full blue flame.

Again, it depends on your needs, so consider peoples' input with that in mind.  Best of luck,

Dear Brad,

Thank you for the inquiry,

I will reply to your questions personally.

1) There was a time when we at GATT-GEN, LLC were offended when the word candle was used to describe the G-MICRO PSL. But over time we became aware that in most peoples mind Wax = Candle period. And that was true for many years until now when our US and Intl Patent Pending Wax Gas Generating System (WGGS) became a reality. Then comes the hard work of teaching people that Wax can be much more then a Candle and is a very potent high density, safe alternative fuel source. To answer your question the G-MICRO PSL is not a Candle and is different in many ways the most obvious is it that it does not have a consumable wick like a wax candle and it has a metallic burner element (watch our video) that burns paraffin wax gas. Yes the WGGS produces volumes of highly volatile combustible gas from solid wax. As a matter of fact, because of the G-MICRO PSL's small size, one of our greatest engineering challenges was to throttle back gas production enough to produce a clean soot free flame.

2)  If the flame is set too high yes you will get some soot on the pot but this is normal for any high molecular weight fuel. However,  if the flame height is adjusted correctly the mostly white / blue flame will not deposit soot. When fitted with a glass lantern globe and the flame is not covered with a pot there is absolutely zero soot.

3)  My favorite question reliable cold weather operation ..... awesome! Blows every other stove away especially if it's used with the DYI wind breaker on our Tips n Tricks page. There is no equal you will love it hands down!

Like any stove device a wind screen is a good tool to have outside if there is wind. Especially on a non pressurized stove. But, having said that once warmed up to full operating temperature the G-MICRO will take a surprisingly stiff cross wind especially if a pot is on top. If its windy I have lit the capillary wick under my parka before exposing to wind. This is only possible because, unlike any other small stove, the wax fuel is hardened and will not spill or flair up. Great feature to have and only available on the PSL.

Inventors note; The G-MICRO PSL is not intended to replace the present crop of turbocharged pressurized gas stoves when used under normal circumstances. They are excellent stoves having a much higher BTU mostly due to their much larger physical size. However, for the uncompromising minimalist who wants to toss a G-MICRO and fuel in his / her pocket and is content with waiting a few more minutes for 2 cups of water to boil then later use it for illumination / lantern there is no equal. For an Explorer like Todd Carmichael  www.subzerosolo.com  (check out his photo page) who carries two G-MICRO PSL's in his kit it's an enabler. Or just someone who wants the security of having a fire making device that can be fueled with candle wax chips in calamity, disaster or vehicle break down it's a life saver.

With Kind Regards



Raymond M. Gatt

GATT-GEN, LLC
