Is Autistic Communication a Language in Itself?

From Lisa Jo Rudy, About.com Guide   November 3, 2010


Last night, I received an interesting response to my blog about a "Communication Shutdown" run as a fundraiser for autism.  Here's the response, from an adult with autism:

I participated in the Shout-Out, being autistic... Even when I was unable to speak as a little kid, according to my parents (both likely autistic), I communicated clearly with my eyes and actions; as an adult, while my non-autistic ex couldn't 'read' my non-verbal communication, my autistic ex definitely could.

The shutdown reinforces the myth that just because non-auties don't natively understand us, we must not be communicating. It's like declaring that because I only know English, immigrants must all be yammering nonsense words. Imagine how silly someone would look to you if they did that!

Both auties and non-auties only natively communicate with our own kind, have to learn the other, and don't pick it up much easier as babies. Most autistic folk learn what we can for survival, some non-autie parents do, too. That's a lot better than deciding that the only "real" language is one's own, and promoting that idea as "awareness" of the people that only speak the other kinds...

My immediate response was confusion. My own son, who has high functioning autism (not Asperger's), has always had a tough time when placed in a group of kids with autism. Kids with autism are often louder, more perseverative, and more likely to tantrum than typical kids. As a result, a classroom filled with children on the spectrum is unlikely to be a relaxed, creative, or even predictable place. Even years after leaving that environment, Tom has been wary of groups of people on the autism spectrum - because he's concerned they'll "bully" him (his interpretation of perseverative name calling) or make very loud noises.

But after thinking about this overnight, I realize that the commenter isn't talking about hanging out in groups: he's talking about one-to-one communication. His suggestion is that the "language of autism" can be likened to "Italian," in that it's a particular way of communicating that can be predicted, replicated, and understood on its own terms by native speakers. He even suggests that some non-autistics can learn the language well enough to be fluent.

I have no idea whether there really is a "language of autism" that's shared by and understood by a large group of people. What's your take on this intriguing idea?

I am endowed with Asperger’s Syndrome.

I doubt there is a “language of autism”. I do suspect that some autists are much more perceptive about certain aspects of body language than a neurotypical.

I am well aware neurotypicals are looking at facial expressions and body language to determine emotional states. I look at eyes to figure out what the other person is looking at, with special emphasis on the fleeting glances. I don’t think autists care about any emotional state other than extreme anger, fear or aggression, and they are especially attuned to whether someone is glancing at them.

After a few minutes of watching, I have instinctively separated people into a spectrum of those who lack sophisticated awareness of their surroundings, (the very neurotypical) and those who are tuned in to the mélange around them. I have consistently found a strong correlation between these qualities and intellectual curiosity. Essentially, without a word being spoken, I can walk away from the random subway car and have given everyone on board an IQ test, which I know from experience of interaction with random people to be fairly accurate.

I suspect that communication with autistics can be manipulated. I recall my mother saying that when she discovered that my Aspie brothers was capable of talking, she ignored his needs no matter how much he fussed, and he soon started talking in complete sentences. I have taught both of my wife’s cats to “talk” to me if they want something; they have to come to me, “talk”, show me what they want by going right up to the door or their food bowl, and “talk” again.

I tailor my E-Mails to those I suspect to be Aspies to cover only a single subject; I know from esperience that only the first subject presented in an E-Mail will be noticed, and I don’t ever trust someone to walk away from a conversation remembering anything.

I don’t think it is a separate language, but like dialects and accents, it can tell you where they have come from.

I don’t think there is a “language” because language is socially constructed. The Deaf aren’t born understanding a universal sign language- groups of Deaf people gradually created sign language; and different groups have created different languages (eg: American Sign is quite different from British Sign (and not in the occasionally-amusing way American English and British English differ)

But, I do not think the writer is imagining something in regards to how Autistics may connect. I would liken it however to the “gaydar” referred to in queer culture- an instinctual awareness about behaviours related to issues/stimuli/etc that are very important to us, or that require our careful attention, things that are unimportant to the average person and therefore the behaviours connected to them are missed (or misunderstood).

November 4, 2010 at 12:55 pm (4) Malia says:

I’m inclined to agree with Alan on this one. A phenomenon my son’s therapists labeled “overselectivity” very obviously played a key role in many of his difficulties with developing language. He had a tendency to get so mired down by the individual details of objects that he wouldn’t classify and sort them into larger groups with common characteristics.

For example, to him, the term “dog” for example wasn’t a generic term encompassing all sorts of sizes and varieties of dogs. It was the specific name of the specific dog we first pointed out to him as being a dog. All other dogs were different in some even minute detail from that dog, so they simply could not be dogs as well.

Teaching him to group things based on “our” generalities was one of the toughest tasks we had as part of his speech therapy; and I would classify these concepts as being “social constructs” in language. Moreover, although they differ a little form language to language, the use of “general” nouns and group classifications seems to be a construct that is somewhat common to most of the main languages used around the globe. For example, although English speaking people use “dog” and Italian speakers use “cane,” both speakers are still working with a word that describes similar animals and that are descriptive of more than one specific dog or breed of dog.

Once my son mastered that single concept, his language development literally took off. As a result, I find it perfectly believable that autistics may socially connect one on one with each other using attention to minute details that are important to them which NTs seldom notice.
