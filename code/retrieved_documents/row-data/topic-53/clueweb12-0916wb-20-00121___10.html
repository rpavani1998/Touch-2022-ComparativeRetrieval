Levi Says “Take Me Fishing”

Published November 29, 2011 | By LifeWithLevi

I loooooooooove to go fishing. In fact, it’s one of the things Jason and I did on our honeymoon.

Minnesota is the land of 10,000+ lakes, so it’s no surprise that fishing is a big sport around here. We have a lake right by our house, so of course I couldn’t wait for the chance to take Levi on his first fishing trip.

I recently took the Angler’s Legacy Pledge, which involves sharing my love of fishing with one new person each year. Who better to share it with than the center of my life?

As an Anglers’ Legacy Ambassador,
I pledge to share my love of fishing
with at least one new person each year;
to pass on traditional skills and earned knowledge;
to pay forward the thrill of fishing
with friends and family,
my love of the great outdoors and my
commitment to a healthy environment;
and to give back what someone once gave me –
the special gift of fishing.

What a lot of people don’t know is that the funds raised from the sale of fishing licenses goes to support natural resource conservation efforts. See? Drinking beer on a boatDOES do some good!

Of course, there was no beer for Levi and I – just some great gear fromBass Pro Shops and a gorgeous Minnesota lake.

I was able to teach Levi about all the cool gear involved in fishing, like this coolShakespeare Ugly Stik Rod & Reel combo.

He was pretty fascinated with the different bobbers, too. Word of caution – small round bobbers are a choking hazard for curious toddlers.

Jason came out to the lake with us to help. He held Levi while I cast the line, then brought him over so he could “help” me fish. Jason was also in charge of the most important part – taking pictures!

With so many lakes in the area, I know that next summer, when Levi’s a bit older, we’ll spend even more time fishing, especially out on our pontoon boat.

One of the best parts about fishing is that it can be a fairly inexpensive hobby. Here in MN, you can buy a family fishing license, which saves on the cost of buying individual licenses for everyone (not that Levi needs one at his age yet), worms are cheap, and you can pick up a rod, reel, hooks, and other basics for minimal cost at outdoor stores likeBass Pro Shops.

More important than the cost savings for a family outing, though, are the lifelong memories I’ll get to share with my son. I can’t wait to get back out to the water in the spring with Jason and Levi. We’ll have to make good on the Pledge – someone has to take photos of all of us fishing together, right?

The Secret To A Flaky Pie Crust

Published November 28, 2011 | By LifeWithLevi

The secret to a flaky pie crust is all in the butter.

Homemade pie crust is super easy to make. All you need are 3-4 ingredients: Flour, butter, salt, and sugar (optional).

The key to getting your pie crust to turn out nice and light and flaky is in keeping everything cold. Your water should be ice cold to start. Your mixing bowl should be chilled. And your butter should be frozen.

That’s right, frozen. Frozen butter makes great pie crust .

There are two methods to getting the butter just right for your homemade pie crust. I’ll show you both ways here.

Jen’s Flaky Pie Crust

Ingredients

  • 1 1/4 C Flour
  • 1/2 C Unsalted Butter (1 stick), frozen
  • 1/2 C Water, chilled
  • 1 tsp. Salt
  • 1 tsp. Sugar (optional)

Directions

In a chilled mixing bowl, combine the flour, salt, and sugar. Place the bowl back in the refrigerator.

Take your butter out of the freezer and chop it into tiny little pieces. As I mentioned before, there are two methods of doing this.

1) The cheese grater method

Use a dishtowel or potholder to hold the butter as you grate it on your cheese grater’s largest setting. This will help prevent melting from the warmth of your hands.

As little piles of butter flakes build up, use a knife to transfer them onto a plate and stick them back in the freezer. Continue this process until the whole stick of butter has been grated.

2) The chopping method

As you can imagine, you don’t need a cheese grater to get small chunks of butter. You can use your regular knife to chop it up into small pieces.

Just be mindful that you’re chopping frozen butter, which is much harder than room temperature or refrigerated butter (aka, be careful with your knife).

I like to use my chopping knife from Shenzhen Knives for this step. The ceramic blade doesn’t stick to the butter, which is important since I want to cut through it as quickly as possible without touching it to get it off the knife (that transfers heat, which will make the butter melt.) It’s also very lightweight and easy to cut with.

Once your butter is chopped into small cubes, place it back in the freezer for a minute or two.

Grab your bowl of dry ingredients and your butter. Add the butter to the dry ingredients by “cutting in” – adding the butter a little at a time and using a knife or spatula to cut it apart as you mix it.

After your butter has been mixed in, slowly add your cold water to the mixture, 1 Tablespoon at a time. Add just enough water to make a dough ball that isn’t too sticky.

Flour your work surface and quickly knead your pie crust dough until it starts to smooth out. It’s helpful if you chill your hands before working with the dough.

Lightly flour the dough ball and cover it with plastic wrap. Allow it to chill in the refrigerator for at least one hour, up to 12 hours. (I usually skip this step and my pies still turn out great.)

Roll out your pie crust on a generously floured work surface. This recipe is supposed to make one 9” pie crust, but I find that it makes one nice bottom crust, and just enough for a very loose lattice on top. You can freeze your pie crusts to use later or use immediately for a delicious, flaky treat.
