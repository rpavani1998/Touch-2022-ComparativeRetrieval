Indoor Succulent Gardens


December 9, 2011

Indoor Succulent Gardens

During these cold winter months, the gardener inside of me still wants to work with plants. The holiday season is a great time to add a few permanent or temporary houseplants to your home. For most, buying houseplants are for decoration, but for many gardeners like me, it's therapy. In addition to the usual foliage house plants, typical holiday plants include the favorites: poinsettias, amaryllis, cyclamen and Christmas cactus.

I grow a lot of succulents that aren't cold hardy in the Texas Panhandle. For the past several years I've been lucky enough to winter them over at the Amarillo Botanical Gardens in their bright and sunny gallery. A few I bring inside my home.

Succulents can be wonderful winter houseplants and require light from an east or south facing window and little care. This seems like a perfect arrangement to me. From around November 15th until February 15th the light is low enough in the sky and angles through the south side windows. I have a few rooms with good southern exposures that are perfect. Even an eastern exposure unobstructed by trees provide enough light to pull them through the winter. Counter space below a skylight or solar tube works good too.

Succulents that are easy to grow inside include members of these plant families:

  • Crassulacea: Aeoniums, Echeverias, Kalanchoes, Sempervivums andSedums
  • Cactaceae: that is, the cacti with many and varied genera and species
  • Mesembranthaceae: Lithops and Mesembryanthemums, better known living stones and look like elephant toes
  • Euphorbiaceae, an extremely large family of plants from pointsettia, crotons, to plants that are cactus look-a-likes, but are not cactus, such as the pencil cactus,Euphorbia turicalli. Euphorbias have a milky white sap that contains latex and other substances that can be toxic and a skin irritant.

These succulents and more are easy to find at nurseries and garden centers as well as on-line shopping. Prices range from about $2 on up, depending on size. They are easy to propagate from leaf cuttings. You don't even need to take a cutting. Often through handling, a leaf will become dislodged. Just pot this up by sticking it in the soil and keep it moist. In no time at all tiny root hairs will begin to grow.

Dish gardens, shallow tabletop containers, make wonderful displays of succulents. Arrangements are only limited by your imagination. Group several of one type of plant, or a mixture. Include a rounded or mounding shape ( Echeveria or Aeonium), with a spiked or pointed plant (Aloe, Agave or columinar shaped cactus) with a draping or hanging plant, such as aSedum. One dual purpose way to display succulents and keep them in their original container is by grouping them together inside a Woolly Pocket ( www.woollypocket.com) until the growing season begins again for planting outside.

Decorating them for the holidays is easy by adding ornaments or winter sprigs to the container. Some people prefer desert motifs, or just a single succulent in a pot.

Whatever your pleasure, your potting soil needs sharp drainage, that is, avoid soggy soil. Succulents will die very fast from over watering during the winter, as their growth is slower. They require little organic matter. Cactus and succulent mixes are available, but you may want to mix your own. Here's a potting soil recipe from Fine Gardening:

5 parts perlite
4 parts bagged potting soil
1 part coarse sand
Pinch of rock dust

Top-dress the container soil with small river rock, gravel, aquarium stone, or a fine-grade roofing gravel to keep the crowns of the plants from rotting ( http://www.finegardening.com/how-to/videos/succulent-potting-soil-mix.aspx). Top dressing the container is a fancy term for mulch. I favor smooth black pebbles or colorful glass stones available in most craft and hobby shops.

The Fine Gardening site recommends lowering the alkalinity of your water. Add one tablespoon vinegar (white vinegar will do) or citric acid to 5 gallons of water. The Cactus and Succulent Journal reported in the September-October 2008 issue, that by adjusting the water's pH, growth is improved, mimicking the acidity of rain water to around 5.6 pH. They observed better coloring, better leaf structure and flowering in addition to faster growing than with using regular tap water, which is usually more in the alkaline range. Adding a bit of acidity to our city water for watering any plants is recommended. Follow the guidelines, don't over do it on the vinegar. If your water is more alkaline than most, adding up to 3 tablespoons of vinegar or citric acid may be required. If you are unsure of your water's pH, test with a pH meter, pH strips or call your city water department to find out.

Warning, you may become hooked on growing succulents. Becoming a member of the Cactus and Succulent Society will increase your scope and knowledge of succulents around the world leading to an expensive hobby. Information about joining can be found atwww.cssainc.org. Austin, Texas will be hosting the 35th Biennial CCSA Convention on June 15th to 19th, 2013. This is the closest city to Amarillo their convention will probably ever be held.

Whether it's one echevaria or cactus or a greenhouse full, just have fun.

Angie Hanna, plain old gardener
