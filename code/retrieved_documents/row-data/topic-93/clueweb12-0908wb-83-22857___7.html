What you can do to protect the health of the Great Lakes

Published: Thursday, October 06, 2011

Tweet








0


By Jim Bloch
For the Daily Tribune

Click to enlarge

Rebecca Hammond displays green products that may be used for cleaning - vinegar, baking soda and peroxide. She uses the siphon hose she is holding to drain bathwater into rain barrels to water her garden. (Jim Bloch/For the Daily Tribune)

The problems facing the Great Lakes, including critical local links such as the St. Clair River and Lake St. Clair, sometimes seem overwhelming. Where do you start when you're trying to deal with the eminent invasion of Asian carp, persistent toxins throughout the Great Lakes basin, the loss of fish and wildlife habitat, especially wetlands, pollution related to runoff from the land and the ongoing threats of massive diversions of water from the lakes to other states?

Start literally in your own backyard and in your own house, says Rebecca Hammond, of Ferndale, a member of the South Eastern Michigan Group of Sierra Club. Hammond spoke to Blue Water Sierra Club in Port Huron on Sept. 28.

The importance of the Great Lakes to the region, the country and the world can hardly be overstated. The lakes represent more than 20 percent of the world's fresh surface water. They provide drinking water for 30 million people in the basin.

At 3,200 miles, Michigan has the longest fresh water shoreline of any state and the second longest in the country - Alaska is first. Michigan is home to 11,000 lakes, 56,000 ponds and 35,000 rivers.


"We're awash in water here," said Hammonds. But water hasn't always been a resource that we've properly protected.

There are around 185 invasive species in the Great Lakes region, including zebra mussels, sea lamprey and the plants such as phragmites and purple loosestrife. Nearly half of those have arrived via ocean-going freighters. Recreational boaters sometimes play an unwitting role in spreading invasive species. If you're a recreational boater - a $9.4 billion per year industry in the region, providing 60,000 jobs - there are a number of actions you can take to prevent the spread of invasive species.

"Always remove any visible mud, plants, fish or animals from your boat before transporting it to another body of water," Hammond said. "Drain all water from your equipment and make sure you clean and dry your boat."

The primary persistent toxins in the lakes are PCBs, DDT, dioxin and mercury. The first three are legacy pollutants, the use of which has been prohibited, but which don't go away. Mercury, however, is a key byproduct of coal-burning power plants which dot the local waterways. The Great Lakes basin is also inundated with polluted runoff from farms and cities in the form of fertilizers, pesticides, herbicides, pharmaceuticals and potentially dangerous bacteria.

Hammond recommended reducing or eliminating the use of pesticides and herbicides at home. One of the ways to do that is dig up lawns and instead cultivate native plants with long root systems that are naturally resilient. Hammond does not have a lawn at her Ferndale home.

"Use non-toxic cleaning products such as baking soda and vinegar," Hammond said. "A mix of baking soda and peroxide will take stains out of anything - it's a miracle cleaner."Continued...

That's the cheapest way to go, she said, but plenty of green cleaners are now available in supermarkets.

"Carefully dispose of oil, paint and solvents," Hammond said. "Don't burn your trash. And don't flush pharmaceuticals down the toilet. Traces of pharmaceutical drugs have been detected by federal health officials in 80 percent of 139 streams and rivers they tested in 30 states, including Michigan."

Wetlands provide habitat for fish, migratory birds and other wildlife.

"They also act as the kidneys of the Great Lakes," said Hammonds, slowing and cleaning the rush of water from the mainland.

According to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, the total amount of wetlands in the United States dropped from 221 million acres in the early 1600s to 103 million acres by the mid-1980s. Michigan has lost between 50-85 percent. Some experts estimate that an acre of wetlands is lost to development every minute. Hammond advised people to lobby local officials to protect their wetlands and organize local restoration efforts.

By and large, the Great Lakes Compact, ratified by Congress in 2008, protects the basin from large-scale water diversions. But it has a loophole. Water may be removed in containers smaller than 5.7 gallons. Who would do that on a large scale? The bottled water industry, said Hammond.

"Don't buy bottled water," she said, also noting the huge waste generated in the form of plastic bottles. "Drink tap water."

Support your municipality's efforts to separate storm and sanitary sewers and to build retention tanks to hold and treat stormwater during large rainfalls before dumping it into the river or lake.

Conserve water and avoid dumping used water back into public sewer systems, she said. Install water efficient hardware and appliances in your homes and businesses. Hammonds drains her bathwater into rain barrels using $3 hoses and a hand pump meant to siphon gas. One bath equals one barrel of water. Connect your downspouts to rain barrels to water your property. A 1,000 square-foot roof generates 420 gallons of water for every inch of rain - about seven barrels full. Plant trees and install rain gardens to slow, absorb and clean rainwater.

Support local, state and federal efforts to clean up the Great Lakes. According to Michigan Sea Grant, a 10-30 percent restoration of the lakes would trigger $7.2-13.2 billion in long-term economic benefits for Michigan and as much as $31 billion for the basin.Continued...
